# **Inside Theory Ventures: Tomasz Tunguz on the AI Revolution and GTM Disruption**

## **Executive Summary**

The transition of Artificial Intelligence from a novelty to a functional "employee" represents the most significant shift in the technological landscape for 2025 and 2026\. This briefing explores the insights of Tomasz Tunguz, founder of Theory Ventures, regarding the current state of AI adoption, the infrastructural constraints of the industry, and the fundamental shifts in go-to-market (GTM) strategies.

**Critical Takeaways:**

* **The Agentic Shift:** 2025 is identified as the year "agents that actually work" will emerge, shifting the IT department's role from managing hardware to managing vast numbers of AI agents—a role Tunguz likens to modern HR.  
* **Infrastructure Constraints:** Energy, rather than just silicon, has become the primary bottleneck for AI. Data center power requirements are scaling from 5 megawatts to upwards of 30 gigawatts.  
* **Economic Paradoxes:** Jevons Paradox suggests that as the cost of AI inference drops (1,000x in the last three years), total demand and consumption will exponentially increase rather than decrease.  
* **Enterprise Adoption Hurdles:** While consumer adoption is near the billion-user mark, enterprise adoption is slowed by the "chaotic and non-deterministic" nature of AI, requiring new architectures to manage error and narrow the range of acceptable outcomes.  
* **Investment Focus:** Theory Ventures targets companies addressing "toil" in markets with labor shortages, specifically focusing on the "Decade of Data," AI as a platform, and decentralized infrastructure.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **State of the AI Market: Adoption and Evolution**

### **Consumer vs. Enterprise Divergence**

The trajectory of AI adoption differs significantly between the consumer and enterprise sectors:

* **Consumer Sector:** Adoption is rapid, with approximately 75% of 18-to-24-year-olds defaulting to generative search. ChatGPT is approaching one billion monthly active users (MAU), leading to a destabilization of the traditional SEO/SEM market.  
* **Enterprise Sector:** Adoption is in earlier stages due to the "narrower range of acceptable outcomes." Unlike consumer use cases where slight inaccuracies are tolerable, enterprise applications require precision (e.g., specific brand adherence and machine-readable JSON outputs).

### **Technical Challenges: Managing Non-Determinism**

Tunguz identifies two core properties of AI that complicate enterprise integration:

1. **Chaotic Nature:** Unpredictability in output.  
2. **Non-Determinism:** Asking the same question twice often yields different results, unlike classic deterministic programming.

To mitigate these issues, four primary techniques are being deployed: | Technique | Description | | :--- | :--- | | **Deterministic Evaluation** | A non-AI system (rules-based) judges the AI’s output. | | **Dual-AI Oversight** | Utilizing a second AI model to evaluate the output of the first. | | **Mechanical Turk (Majority Vote)** | Running the same task across multiple models (e.g., Claude, Gemini, OpenAI) and taking a "two out of three" result. | | **Chain of Thought** | Forcing the model to be introspective, pause, and explain its reasoning steps before providing a final answer. |

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The Infrastructure Crisis: Energy and Compute**

### **The Power Constraint**

The conversation around AI has shifted from GPU availability to energy availability. A single GPU consumes approximately 100x the energy of an electric car. This has led to:

* **Massive Capex:** Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Meta) are deploying 70–80 billion annually in capital expenditures.  
* **Energy Sourcing:** Significant moves toward nuclear energy, including the revitalization of Three Mile Island and the push for modular nuclear reactors, to meet the 5–30 gigawatt requirements of next-generation data centers.

### **Inference and Small Language Models (SLMs)**

The cost of inference has dropped 1,000x in three years, with another 1,000x reduction expected. This leads to the following trends:

* **Small Model Dominance:** Smaller models (2–4 billion parameters) like Microsoft’s Phi or Google’s Gemma are becoming increasingly potent.  
* **Cost Efficiency:** There is a 100x cost difference between the smallest and largest models (450 billion+ parameters). Enterprises are increasingly favoring small, accurate models to achieve ROI.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Disruption of Go-To-Market (GTM) and Advertising**

### **The End of SEO/SEM Stability**

The rise of AI search platforms like Perplexity and OpenAI's search features is disrupting the $250 billion search engine marketing (SEM) industry.

* **Key Question:** Marketers are currently unsure how to influence AI answers or "inject" brand information into the AI’s context window effectively.  
* **Generational Shift:** Younger generations are moving toward video and generative search, leading to social media advertising surpassing search engine advertising for the first time in 2024\.

### **Identifying "Toil" as an Investment Signal**

Theory Ventures utilizes a three-part framework to identify viable AI software investments:

1. **Toil:** Presence of repetitive, boring work.  
2. **Labor Shortage:** A labor market that cannot supply enough humans to perform said work.  
3. **Hiring Desperation:** Managers who are willing to accept an 80% accurate automated solution because they cannot fill the roles.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Theory Ventures: Philosophy and Operations**

### **Fund Structure and Growth**

Founded by Tomasz Tunguz (formerly of Redpoint Ventures), Theory Ventures focuses on 1–25 million investments in early-stage companies.

* **Fund I (2023):** $238 million.  
* **Fund II (Nov 2024):** $450 million.  
* **Portfolio Strategy:** Highly concentrated, aiming for approximately 15 companies per fund.

### **The Intelligence-First Model**

The firm operates with a small team of seven, including a dedicated "Intelligence Team" led by Lauren Deie (formerly of Palantir).

* **Research-Driven:** The team produces 10-page papers on specific premises (e.g., the shift from GPUs to ASICs) to inform investment decisions.  
* **Data Architecture:** Internally, the firm stores research in raw text files, leveraging AI to summarize and tag information while using deterministic systems for long-term ARR and market analysis.

### **Investment Buckets**

| Bucket | Focus | Example Portfolio Company |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Decade of Data** | Modern/Post-modern data stacks; turning raw data "ore" into "steel." | **Omni:** A BI tool combining centralized control with decentralized access. |
| **AI as a Platform** | Automating high-toil enterprise functions. | **DropZone:** AI agents that review 100% of security alerts to prevent breaches. |
| **Decentralized Infrastructure** | Viewing blockchains as databases with unique architectures. | **Allium:** Data infrastructure for Web3, providing accounting and audit-ready data. |

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Personal Productivity and The Future of Work**

### **The Evolving Workflow**

Tunguz emphasizes that technology is advancing faster than human ability to integrate it. He cites his own workflow as an example of the impending shift:

* **Dictation and AI Editing:** Using OpenAI’s Whisper model to dictate three times faster than typing, followed by AI "cleaning" to remove filler words while maintaining personal voice.  
* **AI-Augmented Content:** Creating complex presentations by collaborating with an AI on scripts and transitions, then using Midjourney to generate specific slide imagery—reducing a 20-hour task to under two hours.

### **2025 Outlook**

The overarching sentiment for 2025 is one of intense optimism. Tunguz highlights the convergence of improved electrical grids, self-driving technology, and highly sophisticated reasoning models (reaching "PhD level" in math and programming) as the foundation for a novel way of human-computer collaboration.

# **Briefing Doc: Emergence Capital – Navigating the AI Frontier and the Evolution of SaaS**

## **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes key insights from a discussion featuring Gordon Ritter (Founding GP) and Ys (Partner) of Emergence Capital. The analysis explores the current state of venture capital, the transition from traditional SaaS to AI-enabled services, and the strategic frameworks required to identify "unobvious" opportunities in a shifting macro environment.

**Critical Takeaways:**

* **The "Right-Sized" Venture Model:** In an era of mega-funds, "true venture" requires conviction in specific "swim lanes" and a focus on early-stage hits rather than massive capital deployment into late-stage technology buyouts.  
* **The AI Plateau & Data Scarcity:** Publicly available data is "fossil fuel" that has largely been exhausted. The next wave of AI value will derive from private, commercial data and "human-in-the-loop" coaching networks.  
* **ROI on AI:** Success in the AI application layer is measured by three pillars: Cost reduction, Speed to value, and Quality of outcome.  
* **The Evolution of SaaS:** Software-as-a-Service is not dead but is evolving. While workflow remains essential, pricing models are shifting from per-seat subscriptions to outcome-based and volume-driven metrics.  
* **Capital Efficiency Benchmarks:** The success of Veeva Systems—which reached massive scale on only $3 million in burned capital—serves as a blueprint for the current era of nimble, AI-driven businesses.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **1\. The Macro Environment and the State of Venture Capital**

### **The "Death" of VC vs. Evolution**

While reports suggest a downturn in the VC environment due to locked exit markets, the perspective from Emergence Capital is that venture is not dying but bifurcating:

* **Mega-Funds:** These have transitioned toward "technology investing," functioning more like private equity or buyout firms that require numerous hits to produce returns.  
* **True Venture:** Firms that remain "right-sized" to produce high-multiple returns for LPs by focusing on early-stage, high-conviction bets in specific B2B software lanes.  
* **Current Strategy:** The best investors move aggressively when the market looks "darkest," avoiding the crowd and sticking to values-driven conviction.

### **IPO and Exit Markets**

* **2025 Outlook:** Anticipated stability in the IPO market remains uncertain. Factors include fluctuating interest rates, potential inflation-driving policies, and the "2021 valuation" overhang where companies remain trapped by previous high valuations.  
* **Market Stability:** Markets require stability to flourish; the current political and economic climate lacks the predictability necessary for a robust IPO resurgence.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **2\. The AI Investment Framework**

### **Measuring ROI in AI**

To avoid a "race to the bottom" on pricing, AI companies must demonstrate improvements across three specific metrics:

1. **Cost:** Reducing the expense of performing a specific job.  
2. **Speed:** 10x to 100x faster time-to-value compared to traditional software or manual labor.  
3. **Quality:** Producing a superior outcome to what was possible without AI.

### **The "Coaching Networks" Thesis**

Originating at Emergence in 2016, this thesis posits that the most defensible moat is a "perpetual algorithm" that learns from the habits of expert employees.

* **Human-in-the-Loop:** Software should capture the brilliance of employees within a company's four walls to develop proprietary IP.  
* **Stickiness:** Learning from the best (and worst) habits of workers makes the software integral to the business process and highly resistant to competition.

### **Infrastructure vs. Application Layer**

* **Infrastructure (Together.ai/RC):** Early investment focus was on companies solving "hair on fire" problems like compute sourcing, pre-training, and fine-tuning.  
* **The Transition to Apps:** As infrastructure matures, the focus shifts to application layer companies that use multiple models in concert.  
* **Model Routing:** 2025 is expected to see a rise in systems that instantaneously route queries to the best model (Open Source vs. Closed Source) based on performance and price.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **3\. Overcoming the "AI Plateau" and Data Scarcity**

### **The Fossil Fuel of AI**

There is a growing consensus that the harvesting of publicly available data has run its course.

* **Public Data Exhaustion:** General-purpose models have largely consumed the available internet data.  
* **Commercial Data Opportunity:** The next evolution lies in private commercial data, which sits behind firewalls and involves patents and intellectual property.

### **Strategies to Prevent a Plateau**

Emergence identifies four key opportunities for AI companies to push past current limitations:

1. **Engage Experts:** Capture the "Secret Sauce" of a company’s employees.  
2. **Leverage Latent Data:** Utilize unstructured data already existing within organizations.  
3. **Capture in Context:** Learn from how data is used in real-time workflows.  
4. **Secure the IP:** Ensure that model metadata and business processes do not leak into general-purpose open models.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **4\. The Future of SaaS and Business Models**

### **Is SaaS Dead?**

The consensus is that **SaaS is not dead; it is workflow software.**

* As long as humans are critical to the advancement of technology and IP, workflow—the presentation of information and human reaction to it—will exist.  
* Whether it is a chatbot, a form field, or a neural interface, the "recurring per-seat" model may change, but the necessity of software to manage work remains.

### **Shifting Pricing Models**

New modalities are driving a variety of pricing strategies:

* **Traditional PLG:** Some AI-first companies (e.g., Bolt.new) still use per-seat pricing with usage-based gates.  
* **Outcome-Based:** Firms like Mechanical Orchard (modernizing mainframes) price based on successful migration projects.  
* **Volume-Driven:** Customer support AI is increasingly priced per ticket resolution rather than per seat.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **5\. Strategic Case Studies and Firm Methodology**

### **The Veeva Systems Blueprint**

Veeva is highlighted as a "prolific" success story because of its extreme capital efficiency:

* **Efficiency:** The company burned only $3 million to reach a multi-billion dollar run rate.  
* **Unobvious Strategy:** Instead of building a general cloud tool, Veeva vertically integrated "layers of the cake" (CRM, marketing cloud, content management) specifically for the life sciences industry.  
* **Modern Parallel:** Emergence believes the current AI era will allow for more "one-person billion-dollar companies" or hyper-efficient firms that mirror the Veeva model.

### **Emerging Bets**

| Company | Focus Area | Significance |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Together.ai** | End-to-end AI Infrastructure | Fastest-growing infrastructure company; abstracts complexity of model development. |
| **Bland AI** | Horizontal Voice Platform | Targets the "innately human" modality of voice for complex enterprise interactions. |
| **Bolt.new** | AI-Enabled Web Development | Reached $20M run rate in two months; democratizes web app creation for non-coders. |

### **The "Hunch to Thesis" Process**

The firm maintains its edge through a structured partnership model:

* **Thesis-Driven:** New primary theses are developed every 2-3 years (e.g., Horizontal Cloud, Industry Cloud, Coaching Networks).  
* **Equal Partnership:** An "equal partnership" structure encourages younger partners to take risks on non-obvious ideas rather than chasing safe, consensus-driven deals.  
* **Regret Analysis:** The firm acknowledges missing **Figma** (due to over-focusing on revenue over engagement) and **Twilio** (underestimating the power of the developer-as-kingmaker).

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Key Quotes**

# **Analysis of Investment Philosophy and Vertical Integration: Insights from Packy McCormick**

## **Executive Summary**

This briefing document synthesizes the investment philosophy, market observations, and strategic frameworks of Packy McCormick, founder of Not Boring Capital. With approximately $50 million in assets under management (AUM), McCormick has transitioned from a newsletter creator to a venture capitalist, utilizing deep-form writing as a primary tool for investment underwriting.

The core of McCormick's strategy is the **Vertical Integrator thesis**: investing in companies that combine hardware and software to build faster, cheaper, and more efficient systems than legacy incumbents. Key insights include:

* **Investment Thesis:** Focus on "Vertical Integrators" (the SpaceX/Anduril model for all categories) which take "integration risk" rather than "science risk."  
* **AI as "Super SaaS":** A rejection of AI as a "Godlike" entity, instead viewing it as a highly profitable, commoditized tool that enhances efficiency and creates "win-or-take-all" dynamics in existing software categories.  
* **Writing as Underwriting:** Utilizing "literary non-fiction" styles (modeled after Robert Caro) to rederive industry logic and develop a deep intuition for founder quality and market levers.  
* **The Importance of Brand:** In highly technical or commoditized fields (AI, SpaceX, Nuclear), brand becomes a "Veblen good" and a critical moat for long-term dominance.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The Vertical Integrator Thesis**

McCormick’s primary investment focus centers on "Vertical Integrators," a category of startups modeled after the success of SpaceX and Anduril. These companies are characterized by their ability to control the entire value chain of a product.

### **Core Fundamentals**

* **Hardware and Software Synergy:** These companies use both "bits and atoms" to build better, faster, and cheaper products.  
* **Integration Risk vs. Science Risk:** Unlike "Deep Tech," which often bets on unproven science, Vertical Integrators take proven (though cutting-edge) technologies and focus on the risk of integrating them into a superior system.  
* **Superior Economics:** By controlling the system, these companies aim for higher margins and faster growth than incumbents. McCormick argues their valuations should theoretically be higher because they are "slope over intercept" businesses that eventually replace legacy leaders.  
* **The "SpaceX for Everything" Approach:** The thesis applies to massive, stagnant categories including defense, aerospace, mining, energy, and telecommunications.

### **Sector Applications and Examples**

| Company | Sector | Strategic Insight |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Base Power Company** | Energy/Residential | Focuses on battery pack assembly (integration) rather than cell chemistry (science). Uses batteries to bypass the "transmission bottleneck" of the grid. |
| **Earth AI** | Mining | Vertically integrates exploration and drilling. Higher discovery rates than traditional explorers; uses a model that generates cash to fund further rigs without constant equity dilution. |
| **Fuse** | Nuclear Fusion | Focuses on a specific architecture (pulse power generators) to sell radiation services to the government (NNSA) while pursuing the "call option" of fusion power. |
| **SOS Internet** | Telecommunications | A Colombian "vertical integrator" challenging incumbent Telcos by building its own network infrastructure more efficiently. |

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The Intersection of Writing and Venture Capital**

A defining feature of McCormick’s approach is the use of long-form writing as a mechanism for investment diligence and thesis development.

### **Writing as Underwriting**

McCormick views his 100-page deep dives (e.g., on the company Meter) as "literary non-fiction" that serves as a compression algorithm for the rest of his research.

* **Rederiving Logic:** Writing forces the investor to rederive an industry's logic from the beginning, identifying market levers and historical failures.  
* **The "Robert Caro" Approach:** McCormick suggests that deep journalism is the best way to understand an early-stage company—placing oneself in the "room" with the founder to understand the "why now."  
* **Preparing Intuition:** While some investments are made quickly (e.g., a three-hour coffee meeting with SOS Internet), the writing process "prepares" the investor's intuition for future decisions in related spaces.

### **Founder Magnetism and the "Midwit Curve"**

McCormick emphasizes that the founder is a "shortcut for everything else."

* **The Power of Magnetism:** A key founder trait is the ability to make great people want to do their best work. This creates a psychological environment where employees and investors "fear disappointing" the founder.  
* **Founder as Domain Expert:** Exceptional founders have navigated the "idea maze," understanding exactly why previous attempts in their industry failed and why current circumstances (e.g., battery cost curves) make success possible now.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Perspectives on Artificial Intelligence**

McCormick offers a "spicy take" on the current AI landscape, positioning himself as a "slight bearish" observer of the hype while remaining optimistic about the technology's utility.

### **AI as "Super SaaS"**

* **Software, Not Deity:** McCormick rejects the notion of AI as "God or Satan," defining it instead as "incredibly profitable super SAS."  
* **Qualitative Plateaus:** He notes that while models (like GPT-4 or o3) show incremental improvement, they have yet to demonstrate a "human-like" or "Godlike" leap in capability or major research breakthroughs.  
* **Efficiency over Transformation:** AI is seen as a tool for making companies more efficient (e.g., Ramp's data-driven financial decisions) rather than an entity that replaces the need for humans.

### **The Commodity and Brand Dynamics**

* **The DeepSeek Effect:** McCormick cites DeepSeek as evidence that AI is becoming a commodity business—crews finding ways to do the same thing cheaper rather than creating a qualitative leap.  
* **Value Migration:** As intelligence and answers become commoditized, value will migrate to the next layer of the stack.  
* **Brand as a Moat:** In a world of commoditized technical products, brand becomes critical. He notes that OpenAI (ChatGPT) and SpaceX (Starship) have established brands so strong that even children recognize them, creating a "nobody ever got fired for buying IBM" dynamic for the modern era.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Market Observations and "Venture Theater"**

The document highlights a shift in the venture capital industry away from performative diligence toward more substantive discovery.

* **Critique of "Venture Theater":** McCormick criticizes the "analyst class" and the production of "market maps" or "intellectualized" research meant to satisfy GPs or LPs rather than find truth.  
* **The Downstream Capital Gap:** There is a noted "dislocation" in late-stage capital for capex-heavy businesses. However, once a Vertical Integrator proves a specific "single thing" (e.g., manufacturing capability), capital becomes "really easy" to access as the company becomes the "anointed one" in its category.  
* **The YC Request for Startups:** McCormick expresses skepticism toward YC’s "Request for Startups" lists, arguing that if an idea is already on such a list, it may be too obvious or attract too much unproductive attention. He prefers "inbound" insights from founders who are obsessively tracking niche curves.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

# **Briefing Document: The Strategic Growth and Evolution of Saturn**

## **Executive Summary**

Saturn is a Gen Z-focused calendar and social utility platform that has transitioned from a high school student’s tool to a venture-backed enterprise with over $68 million in funding. Founded by Dylan Diamond and Max (co-founder), the company targets the $80 billion market of Gen Z productivity and social connection. Unlike traditional social media, Saturn prioritizes "stickiness" and utility, boasting a daily active user (DAU) to monthly active user (MAU) ratio of over 50%. The company’s core strategy involves capturing users at the age of 13 in high school and building a lifelong relationship as they move through college and into the workforce. Recent milestones include reaching \#1 in the App Store Productivity category and launching a novel time-based advertising unit utilized by major brands like Nike and Uber.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Founding and Investment Landscape**

### **Origins at Staples High School**

The platform originated as "iStaples," a website created by Dylan Diamond for his own high school in Westport, Connecticut. It allowed students to upload schedules and see which classmates shared their sessions.

* **Word-of-Mouth Growth:** The product grew organically, reaching 80-90% penetration at Staples High School before Diamond graduated.  
* **Transition to VC-Backed Entity:** Partnering with co-founder Max—who identified the product’s exceptional retention data—the team expanded the concept into Saturn, focusing on the complicated nature of high school schedules as a "hook" for daily usage.

### **High-Profile Backing**

Saturn has raised over $68 million from a diverse group of "builder" investors who emphasize long-term endurance over short-term viral spikes.

| Investor Category | Notable Names |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Venture Capital Firms** | General Catalyst, CO2, Insight Partners, Inspired Capital |
| **Individual "Builders"** | Jeff Bezos, Mark Benioff, Elad Gil, Dylan Field |
| **Strategic Advisors** | 01 Advisors (former Twitter management: Dick Costolo, Adam Bain, David Fischer) |

**Key Quote:** "I think they're all builders... in a world where a lot of venture capitalists haven't operated in the field as much, we learn everything from go-to-market strategy to building out the first new ad unit in years." — *Dylan Diamond*

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Product Strategy and Metrics**

### **Utility-First Philosophy**

Saturn distinguishes itself from "rocket ship" social apps that fail to monetize or retain users. The philosophy is to build a highly retentive single-player utility (a calendar) that becomes enhanced by a multiplayer social layer.

* **Sticky Product:** Diamond argues that calendars are more frequently viewed than text messages.  
* **Resilience to Trends:** While other social products suffer from a "reverse K-Factor" (users leave when friends do), Saturn remains useful as a personal calendar even if social connections fluctuate.

### **Core Performance Metrics**

Saturn competes on consumer social metrics rather than traditional enterprise calendar benchmarks.

* **DAU/MAU Ratio:** Over 50%.  
* **D30 Retention:** North of 30% for brand-new users; 70% for existing users.  
* **Annual Return Rate:** 90% of new users return year-over-year (e.g., freshman to sophomore transition).  
* **Market Reach:** Millions of users across 18,000 public high schools.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Technological Innovation and AI Integration**

### **Overcoming Data Friction**

The primary challenge for Saturn was structuring the idiosyncratic data of thousands of different school bell schedules.

* **From Manual to AI:** Early growth required manual "hand-to-hand" coordination with school ambassadors to hardcode schedules. Today, Saturn uses AI and agentic crawling to structure data from school websites automatically.  
* **Crowdsourcing via "Waze" Model:** Users can snap a photo of a new schedule (e.g., a snow day or early dismissal), and AI propagates that update to the entire school community once verified by multiple users.

### **The Conversational Interface**

Saturn is moving toward a natural language interface to make calendaring approachable for young users who find traditional "Global Entry application" style interfaces cumbersome.

* **LLM Adoption:** Gen Z are the fastest adopters of LLMs outside of software engineers.  
* **Daily Summaries:** Users can talk to their calendar to receive daily summaries or input events via voice and text, reducing the onboarding process from eight minutes to under two.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Monetization: The "Time-Based" Ad Model**

Saturn has introduced what Diamond calls the "first new ad unit in consumer in years," focusing on the dimension of time and real-world events.

* **Contextual Relevance:** Brands like Nike and Uber run ads that are integrated into the calendar flow. For example, Nike ran a Super Bowl ad on Saturn that offered free t-shirts, seeing such high engagement that they ran out within minutes.  
* **Conversation Starters:** Unlike individualized social media ads, Saturn ads are often communal. Students at the same school see the same event-based ads, fostering real-world conversation during lunch or between classes.  
* **Hyper-Targeting:** Using calendar data, brands can target specific demographics, such as entire high schools or even specific extracurricular groups like a football team.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Gen Z Insights and Long-Term Vision**

### **Understanding the User Base**

Diamond emphasizes that Gen Z has "extremely high standards" for product performance, design, and privacy.

* **Device Constraints:** The app must work on older phones with limited data bandwidth and operate offline.  
* **The "Antidote" to Scrolling:** Saturn positions itself as a productivity partner that encourages real-world activity, contrasting it with the passive "scrolling" consumption typical of platforms like TikTok.

### **The Path Beyond High School**

While high school is the "first touch point," the ambition is for Saturn to be the user's "last calendar."

* **College and Beyond:** The product evolves as the user ages. In college, the focus shifts from a school-wide directory to a personal "find my friends" style list, where users track the availability of 5-8 close contacts.  
* **Complementary to Enterprise:** Saturn aims to handle the personal "graph" (friends and family) while users use traditional enterprise tools for work.

**Key Quote:** "Today we are millions of people's first calendar, but our ambition is to be your last... we want to build a personal calendar for the rest of their lives." — *Dylan Diamond*

# **Analysis of Kalshi: The Rise of Regulated Prediction Markets**

## **Executive Summary**

Kalshi has emerged as a significant player in the financial sector, establishing itself as the first regulated, onshore prediction market in the United States. Following a "brutal" three-year regulatory struggle, the company has recently reached a turning point, catalyzed by a successful lawsuit against the government regarding election markets and a strategic expansion into sports and entertainment. Kalshi operates not as a traditional bookie, but as a derivatives exchange (comparable to the CME), facilitating the trading of "natural risks"—real-world events ranging from economic indices and political outcomes to cultural trends like Spotify charts and the potential TikTok ban. With major brokerage integrations like Robinhood and Webull, and a mission to "rival the stock market," Kalshi aims to become the primary venue for pricing and hedging global risk through the wisdom of crowds.

## **The Genesis and Regulatory Struggle**

The foundation of Kalshi was built on the premise that financial markets should cater to what people actually know and care about, rather than just esoteric instruments like options or bonds.

* **Goldman Sachs Origins:** CEO Tarek Mansour’s experience in the Equity Exotics desk revealed that institutional investors were often seeking exposure to events (e.g., Brexit, elections) but were forced to use expensive, indirect proxies. Kalshi was conceived to provide a direct, transparent marketplace for these events.  
* **The Regulatory "Torture":** To achieve the scale necessary to work with Wall Street (Citadel, Susquehanna) and major brokers (Schwab, Robinhood), Kalshi pursued federal regulation. This process took three years of "banging head against regulated doorsteps" without a product or customers.  
* **The Judicial Breakthrough:** A pivotal moment occurred when Kalshi won a lawsuit regarding election markets. This victory "unleashed" the company, allowing it to expand from approximately 100 markets to over 1,000 and gain control over its own Clearing House, effectively owning the entire exchange-clearing ecosystem.

## **Business Model and Strategic Infrastructure**

Kalshi functions as an exchange rather than a direct-to-consumer app only, mirroring the structure of massive derivatives entities like CME or ICE.

### **Primary Revenue Streams**

| Revenue Source | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Trading Fees** | Standard fees charged on transaction volume. |
| **Interest on Float** | Revenue generated from the significant deposits held following high-traffic events like the 2024 election. |
| **Market Making** | A separate internal fund trades on the exchange to provide liquidity and generate profit. |
| **Data Sales** | A future revenue path involving charging for the unique predictive data generated by the markets. |

### **Distribution Strategy**

# **Navigating Liquidity and Longevity in Private Markets: Insights from 137 Ventures**

## **Executive Summary**

The venture capital landscape has undergone a structural transformation, with elite companies remaining private for significantly longer durations—often 12 years or more compared to the historical average of three to four. This shift is driven by a massive influx of private growth capital, legislative changes such as the JOBS Act, and innovative equity structures like double-trigger RSUs. Christian Garrett of 137 Ventures argues that this "staying private longer" trend allows generational companies to prioritize long-term R\&D and strategic capital allocation over the scrutiny of public quarterly earnings.

Key takeaways include:

* **The Rise of Secondary Liquidity:** Tender offers have evolved from simple employee retention tools into sophisticated mechanisms for "remarking" businesses and providing liquidity without the need for an IPO.  
* **The Power Law in All Equities:** The "power law"—where a tiny fraction of companies drives the vast majority of market returns—is not exclusive to venture capital but persists across the entire investable universe, including public stocks.  
* **The Tech-Government Nexus:** Policy is a primary, yet underappreciated, catalyst for the growth of entire tech sectors, including Fintech, Aerospace, and Defense.  
* **Strategic Concentration:** In sectors like defense and aerospace, value tends to accrue to a "winner-take-all" or "winner-take-most" handful of companies rather than a broad field of competitors.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The Evolution of Private Markets**

### **The Trend Toward Longevity**

In the early 2000s, the typical lifecycle from founding to IPO was approximately three to four years. Today, the average is 12 years or more. Several factors facilitate this extension:

* **Capital Availability:** The "small cap premium" has moved to the private markets. Private equity and venture capital now manage trillions and hundreds of billions in "dry powder," respectively, providing the growth capital once only available via public markets.  
* **Structural and Regulatory Changes:**  
  * **The JOBS Act (2012):** Increased the shareholder count threshold for forced public reporting from 500 to 2,000.  
  * **Double-Trigger RSUs:** This equity structure, pioneered by Facebook, allows companies to issue equity that only settles upon a "second trigger" (typically an IPO or acquisition), helping to manage shareholder counts and tax liabilities.

### **The Benefits of Remaining Private**

Companies like SpaceX and Stripe utilize the private market to make long-term decisions that public markets might penalize:

* **R\&D Insulation:** SpaceX spent years on Starlink and Starship R\&D without the pressure of public investor scrutiny.  
* **Strategic M\&A:** Private companies can execute acquisitions based on a 10-year view rather than immediate earnings-per-share impact.  
* **Shareholder Alignment:** Private markets offer a longer-term, more concentrated shareholder base.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Liquidity Mechanisms: Tenders and Secondaries**

A "tender offer" is a company-managed secondary sale where existing equity (often from employees) is sold to investors. This facilitates liquidity without issuing new primary shares or diluting the company further to raise cash it may not need.

### **The SpaceX Model**

SpaceX is cited as the pioneer of the "semi-liquid" private market.

* **Frequency:** They conduct tenders every six months.  
* **Structural Necessity:** Unlike many startups using double-trigger RSUs, SpaceX uses **single-trigger RSUs** (similar to public companies). These are taxed upon vesting, necessitating regular tenders so employees can sell shares to cover tax obligations.  
* **Capital Efficiency:** Despite a valuation exceeding $300 billion, SpaceX has raised only approximately $10 billion in primary capital over 23 years, largely due to being cash-flow positive and using secondaries to satisfy liquidity demands.

### **Data Bricks and Stripe**

Massive recent tenders for these companies were similarly driven by "cliffs" where seven-year double-trigger RSUs were expiring, forcing a conversion to single-trigger and creating massive tax bills for employees.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The Intersection of Policy and Technology**

Policy is a foundational driver of market shifts. Christian Garrett identifies several pieces of legislation that birthed or accelerated major tech sectors:

| Policy/Event | Impact on Technology Sector |
| :---- | :---- |
| **JOBS Act (2012)** | Increased shareholder limits, allowing companies to stay private longer. |
| **Dodd-Frank (Durban Amendment)** | Created the interchange-based business models that underpin the modern Fintech industry. |
| **Section 2377 (Title 10 US Code)** | Litigated by Palantir; forces the government to prioritize commercial "off-the-shelf" technology over custom-built "primes." |
| **NASA/Geopolitical Shifts** | The shutdown of the Space Shuttle program and Russian aggression in Georgia created a vacuum that SpaceX filled for the US government. |

### **The Hill and Valley Forum**

Co-founded by Christian Garrett, Jacob Helberg, and Delian Asparouhov, this forum serves as a bipartisan bridge between Silicon Valley and Washington D.C.

* **Mission:** To ensure "technological supremacy" for the U.S. and its allies.  
* **Strategic Importance:** In the context of a "second Cold War," the forum argues that technological and industrial prowess—supported by government partnership—is the primary deterrent.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Investment Philosophy and Sector Insights**

### **The Universal Power Law**

Garrett cites a study by an ASU professor showing that only 4–5% of all publicly listed stocks over the last century drove more than 50% of total market returns. Half of all public companies returned less than Treasury bills.

* **Conclusion:** The necessity of finding and concentrating on "generational" businesses is as true in public markets as it is in venture capital.

### **Defense Tech: Overrated vs. Underappreciated**

While the Total Addressable Market (TAM) for defense is measured in trillions of dollars, Garrett offers a nuanced view:

* **Overrated:** The idea that defense tech will mirror enterprise software with hundreds of winners. In reality, value accrues to a very small number of "primes" (e.g., Anduril, Palantir).  
* **Underappreciated:** The role of "generational talent" scaling with these complex businesses (e.g., Gwynne Shotwell at SpaceX, Matt Grimm at Anduril, and Shyam Sankar at Palantir).

### **Regional Market Perspectives**

* **San Francisco:** No longer the *only* center of gravity, but still the AI hub. Markets like Southern California (Aerospace/Defense) and New York (Fintech) are now distinct, mature ecosystems.  
* **Europe:** While often criticized for immature capital markets, this lack of competition allows the rare "winner" (e.g., Revolut) to accrue massive value without being cannibalized by dozens of venture-backed competitors.  
* **Return to Office (RTO):** Garrett views RTO as a "scapegoat" for businesses struggling through a down cycle. He argues that most successful tech companies are effectively becoming "hybrid" entities, regardless of their public stance.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Notable Quotes**

* "Facebook was the beginning of a trend \[of staying private longer\]; it was not an anomaly."  
* "The small cap premium and the small cap universe in the public markets has completely shifted to the private markets."  
* "Markets tend to over-extrapolate the good and the bad... when there’s a dislocation in the market, you make money. When you concentrate on that opportunity, you make a lot of money."  
* "Fundamentally, deterrence has been one of the greatest forces for good."

# **Strategic Overview: Base Power and the Transformation of the Electric Grid**

## **Executive Summary**

Base Power is an energy technology company positioned as the "modern power company of the electric era." Having recently secured a $200 million Series B funding round co-led by Addition, Andreessen Horowitz, and Lightspeed, the company aims to address the looming U.S. energy crisis through the deployment of a vertically integrated distributed battery network. By bypassing the traditional bottlenecks of utility-scale storage—such as transmission congestion and long interconnection waits—Base Power utilizes a "distributed model" to place batteries directly in homes and businesses. Operating initially in the Texas market, the company employs a philosophy of "violent execution" to iterate rapidly on hardware and operations. Their ultimate objective is to provide affordable, reliable power while evolving into a multigenerational, household-name energy brand.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The U.S. Energy Inflection Point**

The founders identify a significant "knee in the curve" regarding electricity demand in the United States. While load growth has historically been marginal (1-2% annually), several factors are now compounding to drive demand at a much higher slope:

* **Electrification of End Uses:** Transitioning from fossil fuels to electricity via heat pumps and Electric Vehicles (EVs).  
* **Industrial Demand:** The massive build-out of data centers fueled by Artificial Intelligence.  
* **Infrastructure Failure:** The existing grid—the "most complex machine in the world"—cannot currently connect growing supply (renewables) with growing demand due to aging utility infrastructure and financial misalignments.

### **The "SpaceX of Energy"**

Base Power identifies a void in the energy sector similar to the aerospace and defense industries a decade ago. While those industries saw the rise of disruptive players like SpaceX and Anduril, the energy industry has remained stagnant. Base Power intends to fill this void by applying high-speed technology development to utility infrastructure.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Strategic Advantage: Vertical Integration**

Base Power operates on the principle of **compounding cost advantage through vertical integration**. By controlling all parts of the stack, the company can drive down the cost of a commodity (electricity) more effectively than fragmented competitors.

| Vertical Segment | Activities Managed by Base Power |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Technology** | Designing battery packs, developing firmware, and software for wholesale market bidding. |
| **Supply Chain** | Managing warehousing, logistics, and assembly. |
| **Deployment** | Handling site layouts, permitting, and performing the physical installation with in-house crews. |
| **Retail Service** | Acting as the Retail Electric Provider (REP), billing customers, and providing maintenance. |

### **Operational Benefits**

* **Speed:** Traditional battery installations take 2 to 6 months; Base Power can complete them in 2 to 4 weeks, and in some cases, the next day.  
* **Cost Reduction:** In-house design allows for "desk installs" that reduce the time a high-wage master electrician must spend on-site.  
* **Customer Experience:** Members deal with a single entity for hardware, installation, and their monthly power bill, rather than multiple uncoordinated counterparties.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Market Selection and Regulatory Strategy**

The company chose Texas as its launch market for specific strategic and regulatory reasons:

1. **Market Structure:** Texas features "Retail Choice," allowing homeowners to choose their electricity provider. This allows Base Power to enter as a Retail Electric Provider (REP) and own the customer relationship.  
2. **The "Islanded" Grid:** Texas operates its own physical grid, unconnected to the rest of the country. This creates acute reliability challenges and supply volatility due to the state’s high volume of wind and solar power.  
3. **Pro-Competition Environment:** The regulatory regime in Texas favors innovation and allows companies to take market share more rapidly than in states with vertically integrated utilities (e.g., California).  
4. **Utility Constraints:** Incumbent utilities are often financially incentivized to spend money (rate basing) rather than save it, leading to a lack of technological advancement.

### **Partnership Model**

Beyond the direct-to-consumer model, Base Power is partnering with regulated utilities (such as Bandera Electric) to deploy storage fleets quickly within their service territories. This provides the utility with flexible capacity (10MW to 100MW) without the utility having to manage fragmented OEMs, software providers, and installers.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Execution Philosophy: "Violent Execution"**

The term "violent execution" (coined by Packy McCormack and adopted by the founders) defines the company’s operational culture. It prioritizes speed, high expectations, and an "aggressive learning mentality."

### **Iterative Hardware Development**

The company avoids "R\&D traps" by earning the right to learn through rapid deployment.

* **Gen 1:** Mix of off-the-shelf and custom parts; primarily outside manufacture.  
* **Gen 2:** Significant custom components; combination of contract and in-house manufacturing.  
* **Gen 3:** Fully custom design; primarily manufactured by Base Power in the U.S.

"You don't know all the things that break when you have 100 batteries in the ground until you have 100 batteries in the ground... just sprint like hell to get 100 batteries in the ground."

### **Growth Metrics**

* **Deployment Rate:** Currently installing 15–20 units per day (approximately one megawatt per month).  
* **Short-term Goal:** Doubling the deployment rate by summer to become the fastest battery developer in the country.  
* **Long-term Goal:** Thousands of installations per day across all 50 states.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Business Model and Revenue Streams**

Base Power acts as an **asset owner and operator**, rather than just a hardware seller. They do not sell batteries; they sell "affordable, reliable power."

1. **Grid Services:** Monetizing the battery fleet by bidding capacity into wholesale electricity markets (e.g., ERCOT).  
2. **Subscription/Service Fees:** Charging an upfront fee (approx. $495) and a monthly service fee (approx. $17).  
3. **Retail Arbitrage:** Selling electricity to the homeowner and earning a margin on the retail power consumption.  
4. **Strategic Partnerships:** Collaborations with home builders (e.g., Lennar) to integrate Base Power at the point of home purchase.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Organizational Culture and Talent**

The company currently employs approximately 80 people in Austin, TX, with a heavy emphasis on in-person work (5 days a week). The team is a "complex coordination machine" composed of:

* **Hardware/Firmware Engineers:** Designing the physical battery packs.  
* **PhD Software Engineers:** Developing algorithms for wholesale market bidding.  
* **Operational Personnel:** Electricians, logistics experts, and field service teams.  
* **Marketing/Brand Specialists:** Building an enduring "energy brand."

The founders emphasize a "high ownership, low bureaucracy" environment, recruiting heavily from top-tier firms like Tesla, SpaceX, and Apple.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Future Vision: The Modern Power Company**

Within five years, Base Power intends to transition from a Texas-centric startup to a national infrastructure provider.

* **Expansion:** Presence in all 50 states.  
* **Infrastructure Depth:** Moving further "up the stack" into utility infrastructure and "down the stack" into deeper home energy services.  
* **Brand Goal:** Becoming a household name that represents a fundamental shift in how people view their utility—moving from a "one-way street" delivery system to a dynamic technology network.  
* **Endurance:** The founders aim to build an S\&P 500 company that functions as critical, multigenerational infrastructure for the country.
