# **Andrew Wilkinson on Business Operations and Life Design** 

## **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes key concepts from [Andrew Wilkinson](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/awilkinson), co-founder of **Tiny**, regarding entrepreneurial strategy, business model selection, human capital management, and the transformative potential of artificial intelligence. It further explores the intersection of high-scale success with mental health and personal fulfillment.

The core philosophy presented focuses on the principle of fishing where the fish are, which advocates for entering uncompetitive, often boring niches rather than highly contested markets. Success is predicated on identifying unfair advantages and building moats through brands, network effects, or high switching costs. Management is framed as the resolution of people problems, requiring rigorous screening and a low tolerance for poor fit.

Technological integration, specifically through artificial intelligence, is currently utilized to automate executive functions and knowledge work, moving toward a future where many roles may be condensed into singular prompts. Finally, the relationship between wealth and happiness is characterized as largely decoupled, with significant emphasis placed on neurodiversity awareness and medical intervention as more effective drivers of well-being than financial accumulation.

## **Startup Strategy and Idea Selection**

A primary mistake for many entrepreneurs is pursuing markets that have high cultural appeal but poor economics. Effective strategy requires a clinical assessment of competition and personal capability.

### **The Principle of Niche Selection**

Following the advice of [Charlie Munger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlie_Munger), entrepreneurs should find small fishing holes with high fish density and low competition. Crowded markets, such as cafes or AI centric startups, lead to lower margins and higher failure rates.

* Avoid markets where dead bodies are prevalent, if many others have failed in a specific sector, such as bars or pizzerias, there is likely an inherent flaw in the business model that a strong management team cannot overcome.  
* Boring is profitable, software that assists with government forms or niche industrial cleaning often yields high returns because fewer competitors wake up wanting to enter those fields.

### **The Baby Weights Approach**

First-time entrepreneurs should avoid high complexity, highly regulated industries like banking or cutting-edge AI.

* Start with simple business models, such as service-based agencies like **Metalab**, to build a success narrative and business muscles.  
* Use initial wins to fund more complex ventures rather than attempting to deadlift 300 pounds on the first day of business.

### **Scale versus Jobs**

A business only becomes a true business when it reaches a scale where the founder is no longer the primary operator.

* Without employees and leads, a venture is merely a job.  
* Lazy leadership involves becoming Teflon for tasks and delegating as quickly as possible to focus on high-value superpowers like sales or design.

## **Business Models and Moats**

The quality of a business is defined by its staying power and its resistance to competition. **Tiny** focuses on acquiring profitable businesses that are difficult to mess up.

| Moat Type | Description | Examples |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Brand | Pricing power derived from customer loyalty and recognition. | **Coca-Cola**, **Tylenol**, **Advil** |
| Network Effects | A product becomes more valuable as more users join the network. | **Letterboxd**, **Dribbble**, **Facebook**, **Instagram** |
| High Switching Costs | The difficulty or pain of moving data and training to a new system. | **Salesforce** |

### **Operational Philosophy**

**Tiny** operates as a holding company, often compared to the internet version of **Berkshire Hathaway**. Their approach includes:

* Maintaining existing management and keeping changes minimal to avoid disrupting successful cultures.  
* Replacing founders only if they wish to leave, using a CEO who aligns with the existing growth strategy of the business.  
* Prioritizing profitable growth over the venture capital model of burning cash, though companies like **Asana** show that venture scale can win on market share, bootstrapped companies can provide superior life outcomes for founders.

## **Management and Human Capital**

Management is essentially the management of people problems. A single bad actor can create disproportionate stress regardless of business health.

* The Firing Heuristic: If a leader thinks about firing someone even once, they should likely do it immediately. Superstars never trigger that doubt.  
* Hiring for Reality: Avoid hiring for potential and attempting to coach people into new versions of themselves. People generally do not change their fundamental natures.  
* The Elephant and the Rider: CEOs will naturally lean toward their previous expertise, such as enterprise sales or organic marketing. Owners must hire individuals whose natural direction matches the needs of the company, as they cannot be easily steered.

## **Artificial Intelligence and Automation**

The current state of AI is compared to the **Palm** Treo phase, a preview of a more powerful, integrated future.

### **Personal AI Stack**

Strategic use of AI involves building a [Rube Goldberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rube_Goldberg) machine of agents to handle executive tasks.

| Tool | Primary Function |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Lindy.ai** | Building workflows and autonomous agents for email and scheduling. |
| **Replit** | Vibe coding and building web apps or sites through natural language. |
| **Limitless** | Recording and querying daily life for tasks and relationship insights. |
| **Claude** | Advanced writing and document synthesis. |
| **ChatGPT** | General problem solving, research, and memory-based health tracking. |
| **Gemini** | Processing large datasets such as extensive medical records. |
| **Perplexity** | Real-time research on meeting participants and industry topics. |

### **Impact on the Workforce**

Significant displacement is expected in knowledge work, including researchers, assistants, and translators.

* Predictions suggest AI models may be smarter than PhDs in any subject by 2027\.  
* Future jobs may focus on human-centric skills that are difficult to automate, such as social connection, humor, or physical activities like pickleball.  
* The immediate strategy for professionals is to become highly proficient with these tools to build wealth and diversify into energy and compute.

## **Wealth, Happiness, and Neurodiversity**

Financial success does not resolve internal anxiety loops. Wealth often results in more stuff, which can affect the owner and increase stress.

* The Comparison Trap: Even multibillionaires compare themselves to peers, feeling a sense of lack when comparing their wealth to individuals like [Jeff Bezos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeff_Bezos).  
* Philanthropy as Reframe: Directing wealth toward foundations can help remove the ego-driven stress of personal accumulation.  
* Neurodiversity: Approximately 30 percent of entrepreneurs may have ADHD. Understanding this as a brain disorder involving poor executive function can reduce self-blame and improve personal relationships.  
* Medical Intervention: For high anxiety individuals, psychological relief may come more effectively through SSRIs (antidepressants) than through any amount of money. Addressing chemical reactions in the brain can turn a mental state from the noise of Times Square into the quiet of a library.

## **Personal Mottos and Decision Making**

Decision making is guided by the principle that easy choices lead to a hard life, while hard choices, such as firing a person or closing a business, lead to an easy life. Success often comes from asking for the amazing and accepting the great as a result.

# **Base44: Solo Founding, Bootstrapping, and an 80 Million Dollar Exit**

## **Executive Summary**

The journey of **Base44** represents a significant shift in the startup landscape, demonstrating how a solo founder can leverage artificial intelligence to build, scale, and exit a company within six months. [Maor Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144), previously the CEO of **Explorium**, founded **Base44** as a bootstrapped project without venture capital funding. The company achieved 1.5 million dollars in annual recurring revenue within weeks of launch and reached a user base of approximately 400,000 before being acquired by **Wix** for over 80 million dollars. This success was driven by a batteries included approach to AI app development, where the platform provides built-in databases, user management, and analytics. The core growth strategy relied on building in public via **LinkedIn** and creating viral incentive loops rather than traditional paid marketing.

## **Company Overview and Product Philosophy**

**Base44** is an AI powered application building platform that allows users to create complex software using natural language. Unlike competitors such as **Lovable**, **Bolt**, **Replit**, and **Vercel**, **Base44** adopts an opinionated, full-stack approach.

### **The Batteries Included Model**

The platform differentiates itself through integrated infrastructure, including:

* Built-in databases and software development kits optimized for Large Language Models.  
* Internal user management and analytics.  
* Seamless integration that avoids the need for third-party API keys or services like **Supabase**.  
* The ability to generate functional, real-world applications including games, websites, and back-office systems.

## **The Origin and Development Process**

The idea for **Base44** emerged from two specific pain points. First, [Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) observed the difficulty his partner faced while trying to build a lead capture website for her art business using traditional drag-and-drop tools. Second, while volunteering for the **Scouts** in Israel, he noticed the organization lacked the budget for expensive software agencies but had significant internal system needs.

### **Solo Founding and Productivity**

[Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) operated as a solo founder for nearly the entire six-month duration, hiring his first employee, a technical product person, only six weeks before the acquisition. To manage the workload as an individual with severe ADHD, he utilized a specific productivity stack:

* **RescueTime** for deep work management and blocking social media distractions.  
* Custom-built tools within **Base44** to automate content creation and multi-platform social media posting.  
* AI-driven code management, where [Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) reported not writing a single line of HTML or JavaScript manually for three months because he was managing teams of AI to write the code instead.

## **Growth Strategy and Milestones**

The growth of **Base44** was entirely organic, as paid marketing efforts, including a 2,000 dollar influencer post and paid advertisements, failed to yield results.

### **User Acquisition Phases**

| Phase | Strategy | Outcome |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| First 10 Users | Direct outreach to close friends and forced sitting-in sessions to watch them use the tool. | Identified core bugs and refined the initial user experience. |
| First 100 Users | A failed initial **Product Hunt** launch followed by sharing the journey on **LinkedIn**. | Gained approximately 50 users and the first paying customer. |
| Scaling to 400,000 | Building in public and an automated incentive loop for sharing content. | Achieved viral growth, at times reaching 4,000 new users per day. |

### **Successful Growth Tactics**

* Incentivized Sharing: Users received extra building credits for sharing their process or their completed apps on social media.  
* Building in Public: By sharing honest updates about profits, technical failures, and the personal journey on **LinkedIn**, [Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) built a loyal community.  
* Hackathons: The company hosted a **4 Good** hackathon which attracted 3,000 teams and secured sponsorships from **Amazon**, **Google**, **MongoDB**, and **Deloitte**.  
* Velocity as Marketing: The rapid release of new features served as a growth engine, creating an environment where the product evolved faster than users could keep up.

## **Technical Infrastructure**

The technical architecture was designed to maximize the efficiency of AI generated code.

### **The Tech Stack**

* Infrastructure: **Render.com** was utilized for cloud services and scaling, bypassing the complexity of managing **AWS** directly.  
* Database: **MongoDB** was selected for its flexible schema, which is better suited for AI generated data structures that change frequently.  
* Coding Environment: **Cursor** and **Base44** itself were used for development.  
* AI Models: A pipeline was established to route prompts to specific models. Claude 3.5 Sonnet was used for UI and initial app generation, while Gemini handled complex algorithmic problems. Smaller models like **OpenAI** o4-mini were used for patching code chunks.

### **AI-Optimized Repository Structure**

[Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) purposefully avoided TypeScript in favor of plain JavaScript and React JSX because models write these more reliably. He engineered high level software development kits to minimize the amount of code the AI had to write per feature, reducing errors and token usage.

## **The Wix Acquisition**

The acquisition by **Wix** was initiated through a meeting with their CEO, [Avishai Abrahami](https://www.linkedin.com/in/abrahamiavishai), following community suggestions that the two companies should partner.

### **Deal Dynamics and Signing**

The deal was valued at over 80 million dollars for the initial payment, with additional upside based on performance metrics. [Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) emphasized that his willingness to walk away from the deal and continue as an independent, profitable entity strengthened his negotiating position. The final signing occurred at 4:00 AM during a period of heightened geopolitical tension, specifically as news broke regarding conflict between Iran and Israel.

## **Strategic Insights for Founders**

The success of **Base44** suggests that the importance of domain expertise is rising while the barrier of technical execution is falling due to AI.

* Velocity over Perfection: Rapid iteration and visible progress are more effective for growth than polished initial releases.  
* Community as Support: A strong, engaged community can act as a DevOps and support team for a solo founder by providing instant feedback on bugs and system status.  
* Internal Tools: Solo founders should build custom productivity tools tailored to their own workflows to maintain high output.  
* Focus on Enjoyment: [Shlomo](https://il.linkedin.com/in/maor-shlomo-1088b4144) attributes his ability to work through the stress of solo founding to the fact that he spent at least half of his time in his genius zone, focusing on product building rather than administrative tasks.

# **The Foundation Sprint: A Framework for Rapid Startup Validation**

## **Executive Summary**

The Foundation Sprint is a 10-hour, two-day framework designed to help founders and product teams identify, refine, and validate startup ideas at the inception stage. Developed by [Jake Knapp](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jake-knapp) and [John Zeratsky](https://www.linkedin.com/in/johnzeratsky/) at **Character Capital**, the process addresses common failure modes where teams lack alignment on customer targets or fail to test their core assumptions. By clearing calendars and following a highly scripted sequence of activities, teams move from vague concepts to a clear, testable founding hypothesis. This framework serves as a precursor to the five-day Design Sprint, ensuring that the underlying strategy is sound before resources are spent on prototyping and execution. Key outcomes include the identification of the target customer, a defined competitive advantage through differentiation, and a prioritized implementation approach.

## **Origins and Purpose**

The Foundation Sprint emerged from the creators' experiences at **Google** and **Google Ventures**, where they developed the Design Sprint and worked with over 300 teams at companies including **Microsoft**, **YouTube**, **Slack**, and **Uber**. While the Design Sprint focuses on building and testing prototypes in five days, the Foundation Sprint was created in 2021 to address the missing manual for the earliest days of company building.

### **The Problem of Generic AI Execution**

A significant driver for the Foundation Sprint is the current trend of rapid product development using AI. While AI tools allow for faster building, products often turn out generic because they are trained on similar data. The Foundation Sprint encourages teams to slow down for deep thinking to identify what actually makes a product unique, preventing the trap where going fast eventually slows a team down in the long run.

## **Phase One: The Basics**

The first phase of the sprint focuses on establishing common ground among the core leadership team, which typically includes those in charge of product, engineering, design, and marketing.

### **Work Alone Together**

The process utilizes a tactic called “work alone together”, specifically the note and vote method. Team members work in silence to write down individual answers to foundational questions before voting on them. This prevents groupthink and ensures the designated decider, usually the CEO or Chief Product Officer, has a range of perspectives to consider.

### **Core Questions**

The team must reach consensus on several simple but critical points:

* **Target Customer:** Identifying the most important user for the product.  
* **The Problem:** Defining the specific customer problem being solved.  
* **Competition:** Identifying how customers currently solve the problem, including workarounds and non-direct alternatives.  
* **Advantages:** Identifying the team's unique capabilities, insights, or motivations that big companies cannot easily replicate.

## **Phase Two: Differentiation**

Differentiation is the heart of the Foundation Sprint. It focuses on the promise made to the customer and how that promise separates the product from alternatives.

### **Classic and Custom Differentiators**

Teams begin by scoring their idea against competitors using classic continuums such as:

* Fast to slow.  
* Free to expensive.  
* Easy to use to hard to use.  
* Integrated to siloed.  
* Focused to one size fits all.

Following the classic analysis, teams develop custom differentiators. For example, a company like **Latchel** (formerly **Lyric**) might differentiate by being cooperative versus the siloed nature of **Shopify**.

### **The 2x2 Diagram and Loserville**

Teams plot their product against competitors on a two-by-two diagram. The goal is to occupy the top-right quadrant. The other three quadrants are collectively referred to as Loserville. To be successful, the product must deliver on a promise that is both achievable for the team and valuable to the customer.

### **Project Principles**

The phase concludes with the creation of project principles, or a mini manifesto. These are decision making guides similar to the **Google** mantra that fast is better than slow. These principles help teams choose between different technical or design approaches during development.

## **Phase Three: The Approach**

The final phase involves evaluating different implementation paths to reach the target customer.

### **Magic Lenses**

Teams use Magic Lenses to evaluate their options through different expert viewpoints:

* **Customer Lens:** Which approach offers the best solution to the customer problem?  
* **Pragmatic Lens:** Which approach is the fastest and cheapest to build?  
* **Growth Lens:** Which approach is the easiest for customers to adopt?  
* **Money Lens:** Which approach creates the most long-term financial value?  
* **Differentiation Lens:** Which approach best aligns with the identified competitive advantages?  
* **Conviction Lens:** Which approach generates the most excitement among the founders?

### **The Founding Hypothesis**

The output of the entire 10-hour sprint is a single sentence hypothesis following this format: If we solve this problem for this customer with this approach, we think they will choose it over the competitors because of differentiator one and differentiator two.

## **Validation and Iteration**

Once the Foundation Sprint is complete, the team enters a sequence of Design Sprints, typically lasting two to three weeks, to test the hypothesis.

### **The Scorecard Method**

During these testing weeks, teams build prototypes and conduct customer interviews. They use a scorecard to track results across multiple variables:

* **Red:** Indicates the assumption was wrong and requires significant changes.  
* **Yellow:** Indicates some promise but needs refinement.  
* **Green:** Indicates a clear signal that the idea clicks with the customer.

### **Prototyping with AI**

In the current environment, teams often use AI for vibe coding, allowing for the rapid creation of realistic looking prototypes. However, the framework emphasizes that while prototyping can be outsourced to AI, the core thinking and messaging must remain with the team to avoid generic results.

### **Case Studies in Validation**

* **Latchel:** An engineering-led team from **Substack** used the sprint to move from a wide range of ideas to a specific social sales app for artisans. Their scorecard shifted from almost entirely red in week one to entirely green by week three.  
* **AxionOrbital:** A solo founder used the process to refine a geospatial development tool, using detailed paper sketches as a form of prompt engineering for AI-generated prototypes.  
* **Bindwell:** Used the framework to identify a price-based differentiation made possible by using AI to design pesticides, a task previously requiring massive manual R\&D teams.  
* **Reclaim:** Focused on the differentiation of automatic AI prioritization for calendars, which eventually led to an acquisition by **Dropbox**.

## **Conclusion**

The Foundation Sprint provides a high ROI by accelerating three to four months of traditional work into a 10-hour window. By forcing teams to be explicit about their strategy and differentiation before they start building, it increases the odds of reaching product market fit. The process not only provides tactical alignment but also brings the team closer to the customer through immediate, evidence-based feedback loops.

# **Every: The AI-Native Startup Strategy** 

This document analyzes the operational models, engineering philosophies, and economic predictions of **Every**, a 15 person startup operating at the intersection of media and software. Led by co-founder and CEO [Dan Shipper](https://www.linkedin.com/in/danshipper), the organization demonstrates a transition toward AI native workflows where manual labor is replaced by agentic delegation and context engineering.

## **Executive Summary**

The analysis of **Every** provides a blueprint for the AI native organization, characterized by a lean team of 15 employees managing five software products, a daily newsletter with 100,000 subscribers, and a consulting arm. Central to this efficiency is the total abandonment of manual coding in favor of an arsenal of AI agents and a dedicated role for AI operations. The findings suggest a shift from a knowledge economy to an allocation economy, where the primary value of a worker lies in their ability to manage and allocate AI models. Key takeaways include:

* The emergence of the “Head of AI Operations” as a critical role for identifying and automating repetitive workflows across an organization.  
* A new engineering paradigm termed compounding engineering, where every unit of work is designed to make subsequent units easier through automated prompt libraries and PRD generation.  
* The prediction that AI will act as a force for reshoring American jobs by making expensive services affordable for small companies through domestic, AI enhanced labor.  
* The effectiveness of a CEO led adoption model, where leadership usage of tools like **OpenAI** o3 or **Anthropic** Claude is the primary predictor of successful organizational integration.

## **The Evolution of AI-Native Operations**

The operational structure of **Every** serves as a case study for high-leverage business models in the AI era. The company maintains a 15-person team that manages a diverse portfolio, including a newsletter, consulting services, and software apps such as Cora, Sparkle, Spiral, and Monologue.

### **The Role of AI Operations**

The organization utilizes a Head of AI Operations to bridge the gap between day-to-day tasks and automation. This role is responsible for:

* Identifying repetitive internal tasks through weekly sit downs with team members.  
* Building custom prompts and workflows that automate these identified areas.  
* Implementing behavioral updates to ensure the team utilizes the developed AI tools.  
* Scaling high-quality editorial standards by codifying an editor in chief's taste into prompts used by engineers and writers.

### **Institutionalizing AI-First Culture**

Adoption within **Every** is driven by an internal requirement that every employee be AI forward. This is exemplified by the editorial process, where style guides are integrated into **Anthropic** Claude Opus 4 to perform initial copy edits. This allows human editors to focus on higher level creative decisions rather than repetitive style corrections.

## **The New Engineering Paradigm**

A significant finding is that the product team at **Every** does not handwrite code. Instead, they use a sophisticated stack of agents to handle implementation, allowing engineers to function more like architects or managers.

### **Compounding Engineering**

The team uses a philosophy called “compounding engineering”. This approach mandates that for every unit of work, the next unit must become easier.

* Instead of writing a Product Requirements Document (PRD) manually for each feature, engineers develop prompts that turn rambling thoughts into high-quality PRDs.  
* These PRDs are fed into **Anthropic** Claude Code to autonomously execute tasks across the file system.  
* This creates a library of shared prompts and automations, often stored on **GitHub**, that increases the team's velocity over time.

### **The Agentic Stack**

The engineering team utilizes a diverse array of agents to provide different perspectives and technical styles:

* **Anthropic** Claude Code: Used for autonomous, agentic work on local file systems, capable of spawning sub-agents for parallel tasks.  
* Friday and Charlie: Specific agents used within **GitHub** for checking pull requests and offering varied perspectives on code quality.  
* **OpenAI** o3: Used for its memory capabilities and high-quality reasoning in writing and one-off coding tasks.

## **The Allocation Economy and the Future of Work**

The source context introduces the concept of the “allocation economy” as the successor to the knowledge economy. In this new era, the most valuable skills are those traditionally associated with management rather than specialized manual execution.

### **Transitioning from Specialist to Manager**

As AI becomes more capable, the cost of management decreases, allowing every worker to become a model manager. Essential skills in this economy include:

* Evaluating talent: Determining which AI model is best suited for a specific task.  
* Vision and Taste: Maintaining a high bar for output and providing the necessary feedback to agents.  
* Context Engineering: Orchestrating the right information at the right time to ensure models produce accurate results.  
* Generalism: The ability to jump between domains (coding, writing, marketing) by leveraging AI knowledge.

### **Acceleration of Entry-Level Talent**

Contrary to fears regarding the displacement of junior workers, the document highlights how AI can accelerate professional development. One example describes a junior writer who achieved a year's worth of progress in two months by recording feedback and integrating it into prompts, ensuring he never made the same mistake twice. "Whenever I see a kid with ChatGPT, I'm like, Holy shit, they're going to go so much faster than any other person that I've worked with."

## **Leadership and Adoption Tactics**

Based on observations from the consulting arm of **Every**, successful AI adoption is primarily top-down. The document identifies specific tactics used by leaders at companies like **Walleye** to drive integration.

### **The CEO Benchmark**

The most reliable predictor of a company's productivity gains through AI is whether the CEO uses a chatbot daily. A CEO who uses the tools can set realistic expectations and drive excitement. "If the CEO is in it all the time, being like, This is the coolest thing, everybody else is going to start doing it."

### **Strategic Adoption Tactics**

Effective leaders employ the following methods to institutionalize AI:

* The AI-First Memo: Explicitly stating the company's direction and demonstrating it by writing the memo itself with AI.  
* Weekly Prompt Sharing: Hosting meetings where employees demonstrate new use cases and successful prompts.  
* Transparency in Usage: Sending weekly emails with usage statistics for tools like **OpenAI** ChatGPT to create awareness and momentum.  
* Rewarding Early Adopters: Identifying the 10 percent of employees who are early adopters and highlighting their work to the rest of the organization.

## **AI Tool Stack and Productivity Heuristics**

The document outlines a personal and professional tool stack that prioritizes speed, memory, and agentic autonomy.

| Tool | Primary Use Case |
| :---- | :---- |
| **OpenAI** o3 | Day-to-day writing, coding, and personal growth reflection due to its memory feature. |
| **Anthropic** Claude Code | Autonomous engineering and processing large sets of local files via the command-line interface. |
| **Google** Gemini CLI | Used for its low cost and high performance within custom applications. |
| **Granola** | Transcription and meeting notes. |
| **Notion** | Meeting recording and organizational documentation. |
| **Anthropic** Claude Opus 4 | Used as a judge for content quality due to its unique gut sense for interesting writing. |

### **Context Engineering over Context Window**

While large context windows are useful, the document argues that context engineering, the ability to provide the right context at the right time, is responsible for at least half of a model's performance. This is critical for tasks like predicting a CEO's response in a meeting or mimicking a specific literary style like that of [Leo Tolstoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy).

## **Business and Financial Philosophy**

**Every** operates under a unique financial model designed to preserve creative risk-taking while maintaining stability.

### **The Sip Seed Model**

Instead of traditional venture capital rounds, **Every** utilizes a sip seed model with backing from **Starting Line VC**. This allows the company to pull down capital as needed at a set cap, providing a psychological safety net without the pressure to burn cash typical of large venture rounds. "I'm not, and the rest of the team is not staring at a gigantic number in the bank account being like, cool, we can burn this."

### **Software as Content**

The organization views the current market as a greenfield where software is increasingly unbundled from general-purpose tools like **OpenAI** ChatGPT. By incubating products that solve their own internal needs, such as Cora for email management, they create a pipeline of apps that appeal to their audience. This model suggests that in the AI era, smaller organizations can build high-impact software with significantly less capital, as evidenced by the $300,000 cost to build Cora compared to the millions it might have cost in a pre-AI environment.

# **Benjamin Mann on Superintelligence, Safety, and the Future of AI**

## **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes key insights from [Benjamin Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann), co-founder of **Anthropic**, regarding the rapid advancement toward superintelligence and the critical necessity of AI safety. [Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann) forecasts a 50th percentile chance of reaching superintelligence by 2028, noting that progress is accelerating rather than plateauing. The document outlines the foundational reasons for the establishment of **Anthropic**, specifically citing a perceived lack of prioritization for safety at **OpenAI**. Central to **Anthropic**'s strategy is the implementation of Constitutional AI and Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) to ensure models remain helpful, harmless, and honest. [Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann) estimates the existential risk, or X-risk, of a catastrophic outcome from AI to be between 0 and 10%, necessitating proactive alignment before superintelligence is achieved. Economically, the transition toward transformative AI is expected to cause significant job displacement, with unemployment potentially reaching 20%, eventually leading to a post-capitalist world of abundance.

## **The Path to Superintelligence and Scaling Laws**

Despite public narratives suggesting a plateau in AI progress, [Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann) asserts that development is accelerating due to improvements in post-training techniques and a faster cadence of model releases.

* **Timeline for Superintelligence:** [Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann) identifies 2028 as the 50th percentile mark for achieving superintelligence. "I think 50th percentile chance of hitting some kind of superintelligence is now like 2028."  
* **The Acceleration Effect:** He compares the current pace of progress to a light speed journey where time dilation occurs, making it difficult for those outside the industry to perceive the true rate of change.  
* **Validity of Scaling Laws:** Scaling laws continue to hold across multiple orders of magnitude. This progress is driven by three core ingredients: compute, algorithms, and data.  
* **The Economic Turing Test:** [Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann) prefers the term transformative AI over AGI. He defines success through the Economic Turing Test, which is passed when an AI agent can be hired for a job for a month and the employer cannot distinguish it from a person. Transformative AI is reached when this occurs for 50% of money-weighted jobs.

## **Organizational Philosophy and the Recruiting Landscape**

The competitive environment for AI talent has reached unprecedented levels, with major technology firms offering massive compensation packages to poach researchers from top labs.

* **The Recruiting Battle:** **Meta** has reportedly offered signing bonuses and compensation packages totaling $100 million to top researchers. [Mann](https://www.linkedin.com/in/benjamin-mann) views these numbers as rational given the immense value even a small percentage increase in model efficiency provides.  
* **Retention through Mission:** **Anthropic** maintains its staff despite these offers by focusing on the future of humanity rather than financial gain. "We've been much less affected because people here, they get these offers and then they say, well, of course I'm not going to leave because my best case scenario at **Meta** is that we make money and my best case scenario at **Anthropic** is we affect the future of humanity."

# **Scaling Innovation: A Framework for Profitable Growth and AI Monetization**

## **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes the core themes and strategies presented by [Madhavan Ramanujam](https://www.linkedin.com/in/madhavansf), a managing partner at **49 Palms Ventures** and former partner at **Simon-Kutcher**, regarding the transition from product innovation to building a durable, profitable business.

The primary challenge for modern founders is architecting a business for profitable growth by simultaneously mastering market share (acquisition) and wallet share (monetization and retention). Many companies fail by operating on a single engine strategy, either pursuing growth at all costs while postponing monetization or focusing on a small customer base without expanding. AI companies face unique pressures, as they must master monetization from day one due to high cost dynamics and the significant value they capture by replacing labor rather than just providing software access. The most effective pricing models for AI correlate with high autonomy and high attribution, moving toward outcome based pricing where companies capture 25% to 50% of the value created.

## **The Architecture of Profitable Growth**

Building an enduring business requires balancing two distinct engines: market share and wallet share. This necessitates proficiency in acquisition, monetization, and retention.

### **The Single Engine Trap**

Companies often fall into the trap of focusing on only one growth engine, which leads to specific organizational archetypes and associated failures:

| Archetype | Description | Common Traps |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Disruptor** | Focuses heavily on acquisition and market share. | Landing but not expanding by giving away too much value for free, and failing to retain or upsell existing customers. |
| **Money Maker** | Focuses primarily on monetization. | Nickel and diming customers with hidden fees, or suffering from the price premium paradox where high prices stifle acquisition. |
| **Community Builder** | Focuses on a small, loyal customer base. | Missing the frontier by failing to attract new customer types, and training the best customers to expect more for less. |

"The good founders need to be able to dominate both market share and wallet share. It is not a choice you need to get better at both."

## **Strategies for the Startup and Scaleup Phases**

A framework of nine strategies helps companies architect toward profitable growth, divided between early stage startups and scaling organizations.

### **Startup Phase: Beautifully Simple Pricing**

In the early days, pricing must be simple to reduce friction in sales conversations. A simple pricing strategy is one that a customer can easily articulate back to the founder.

* **Value Story:** Pricing should contextualize the cost against the value provided. For example, **Superhuman** framed its 30 dollar monthly fee as paying a dollar a day to regain four hours of weekly productivity.  
* **The 20/80 Axiom:** "20% of what you build drives 80% of the willingness to pay but the irony is that that 20% is the easiest thing to build often."

### **Scaleup Phase: Mastering Negotiations**

As companies grow, especially in B2B contexts, extracting full value requires sophisticated negotiation strategies rooted in value selling.

* **Gives and Gets:** Negotiators should never give a concession without getting something in return, such as a value audit where the customer helps document the product's financial impact.  
* **Value Selling:** This involves creating needs rather than just discovering them, using affirmation loops to confirm customer agreement on value, and co-creating ROI models.  
* **Tapering Concessions:** Effective negotiators reduce the size of concessions over time, for example, moving from a 15% discount to 5% then 2%, to signal that the negotiation is reaching its limit.

## **AI Pricing and Monetization**

AI pricing differs fundamentally from traditional SaaS because it often involves higher operating costs and provides direct labor substitution.

### **The Attribution and Autonomy Matrix**

Founders should select a pricing model based on where their product sits regarding its autonomy (human in the loop vs. independent action) and attribution (the ability to prove the product caused a specific outcome).

* **Seat-Based/Subscription:** Best for low attribution and low autonomy (co-pilot mode).  
* **Usage-Based:** Best for high autonomy but low attribution, where usage serves as a proxy for value.  
* **Hybrid (Seat \+ Consumption):** Best for products with clear attribution but still requiring a human in the loop, such as **Canva** or **GitHub**.  
* **Outcome-Based:** The golden quadrant where products have high autonomy and high attribution. "If you want to win in AI, figure out a way to get to that quadrant."

### **The Shift to Outcome-Based Models**

Currently, only about 5% of companies use true outcome-based pricing, such as **Intercom** charging for a **Finn AI** resolution or **ChargeFlow** taking a percentage of recovered chargebacks. In these models, companies can capture 25% to 50% of the value created, compared to the 10% to 20% typical in traditional SaaS.

## **Tactical Execution: Proof of Concept (POC) and ROI**

A common mistake in AI startups is treating a POC as a technical test rather than a commercial one.

### **Reframing the POC**

"A POC needs to be framed, as the purpose of a POC is to build a business case and we are going to co-create an ROI model with the customer as opposed to it being a tech and product functionality feature test."

* **Charging for POCs:** Startups should charge for POCs to qualify leads and filter out those who are merely curious about AI.  
* **Price Deflection:** When pushed for a price before a business case is built, founders should provide a budget range or contextualize the price based on potential ROI, such as a 1 to 10 ratio.

### **Co-creating the ROI Model**

An ROI model should never be presented as a finished document at the end of a trial. It must be built using inputs agreed upon by the customer from day one, focusing on:

1. **Incremental Gains:** Revenue increases or churn reduction.  
2. **Cost Savings:** Headcount reduction or lower license costs.  
3. **Opportunity Costs:** Quantifying what employees do with the time saved by the AI.

## **Key Pricing Axioms**

The following principles serve as high level guides for founders navigating monetization:

* **Price Paralysis:** Reluctance to increase prices is usually internal and emotional rather than logical.  
* **Stopping Churn:** "To stop churn you need to attract customers who won't leave." This involves using data to identify and acquire customer segments with naturally high retention.  
* **Land and Expand:** Founders must ensure that their initial entry product does not give away the entire value proposition, leaving room for future monetization.  
* **Pricing Power:** As noted by [Warren Buffett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warren_Buffett), the true definition of a strong company is its ability to increase prices without requiring a prayer session.

## **Conclusion**

The winners in the AI era will be those who master monetization from the start. By avoiding the traps of single engine growth and moving toward models that emphasize autonomy and attribution, companies can build sustainable, profitable businesses that reflect the high value they deliver to the market. Founders should treat pricing as a continuous process of testing and learning, revisiting strategies at least once a year to align with the rapid pace of AI innovation.

# **Matt LeMay on Strategies for Impact First Product Teams**

## **Executive Summary**

The current economic landscape for product management has shifted from a zero interest rate (ZIRP) environment to one focused on cost savings and direct value. Companies such as **Spotify** have explicitly stated that layoffs are a response to teams performing “work around the work” rather than focusing on high-impact opportunities. To survive and thrive, product managers must move away from focusing on intermediate best practices, such as perfectly cascaded objectives and key results (OKRs), and instead align their work directly with business-critical outcomes.

The primary challenge facing modern product teams is the low impact PM death spiral, a cycle where teams prioritize minor, cosmetic features to avoid scrutiny and risk. This behavior results in increased product complexity and organizational overhead, eventually leading to layoffs when the business can no longer justify the investment. To counteract this, product managers must adopt an impact first mindset by setting goals no more than one step away from top-line company objectives. Success is defined by facilitating a value exchange between the business and its customers, which requires a deep understanding of the specific business model, whether it is a startup seeking growth or a publicly traded company focusing on profitability. By treating organizational constraints as guideposts rather than impediments, individual contributors can drive meaningful impact regardless of the broader company culture.

## **The Crisis of Low-Impact Work**

The industry is currently witnessing an increase in layoffs because many teams fail to provide a clear return on investment. Product managers often assume that because they were assigned a task, it must be critical to the business. However, leadership at companies like **Spotify** suggests otherwise, identifying an excess of teams supporting work instead of driving impact.

### **The Low Impact PM Death Spiral**

The “low-impact PM death spiral” (LIPMDS) is a dynamic observed in medium to large companies where teams choose work that is easy to build and unlikely to cause immediate harm. This process is illustrated by the following stages:

* **Low Impact Selection:** Teams add small features or cosmetic improvements, similar to adding rhinestones to a car. These are visible to executives but do not improve the core engine of the business.  
* **Increased Complexity:** As multiple teams add minor features, the product becomes a collection of loosely connected parts rather than a single experience. This increases technical and operational dependencies.  
* **Organizational Bloat:** Companies add program management layers to manage these dependencies, which makes it harder to perform high-impact work.  
* **The Breaking Point:** The cycle continues until the hood of the car becomes too heavy to lift, making the engine inaccessible, and the business eventually initiates layoffs.

## **The Impact First Framework**

To avoid the death spiral, product teams must transition to being impact first. This involves three fundamental steps aimed at radical accountability and clarity.

### **Step 1: Set Team Goals Close to Company Goals**

Most organizations fail by cascading goals through too many levels, creating a distance between day-to-day work and business success.

| Goal Type | Recommended Structure |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Distance from Top-Line** | No more than one step away from company goals. |
| **Logic** | Every goal should be connectable to the company objective via one mathematical operator or one why statement. |
| **Example** | If a company goal is top-line revenue, a team goal might be converting single-product users to multi-product users, multiplied by the known increase in customer lifetime value. |

### **Step 2: Maintain Impact Focus at Every Stage**

Impact must remain the primary filter during every part of the product building process, including the creation of strategies, epics, and OKRs. Teams often spend weeks perfecting the theoretical harmony of their objectives but fail to ask how those objectives add up to the million-user or million-dollar target set by the CEO.

### **Step 3: Connect All Work to Impact Estimates**

Prioritization should not be based on abstract proprietary scores. Instead, impact estimation must use the same unit of measure as the team goals.

* **Honesty in Estimation:** If a landing page only reaches a few hundred people, its impact score must reflect that specific number rather than an arbitrary high impact rating.  
* **Acceptance of Risk:** High-impact work often involves more dependencies and less certainty. Teams must be brave enough to pursue these initiatives if they are the only path to meeting the business-critical goal.

## **Navigating Organizational Realities**

Individual contributors often use organizational constraints as an excuse for not being impact first. However, these constraints are the commercial realities that should shape the work.

* **Regulated Industries:** Constraints provide a commercial advantage if a team understands them better than the competition.  
* **B2B Models:** These offer immediate access to customers and a clearer understanding of the value system.  
* **Financial Targets:** Quarterly earnings targets provide clear guidance on what the business values most, removing the need for deep cascading.

### **The Role of the Product Manager**

The product manager is not a solo mini CEO but is responsible for the whole team thinking like a CEO. This involves bringing commercial thinking to the group and facilitating the value exchange between the business and customers. Evidence from teams at **Mailchimp** shows that when product managers align their work with commercial fundamentals, such as specific user success rates for sending emails, they can justify subtracting features to streamline the product experience.

## **Stakeholder Management and Trade-offs**

Conflict averse product managers can manage stakeholders effectively by providing options and highlighting trade-offs rather than simply saying yes or no.

* **Access to Information:** Executives may have information about acquisitions or financial challenges that are not public. Product managers should provide the information they would want if they were making the decision.  
* **The Power of Recommendation:** Presenting three options with a specific recommendation is more effective than a single option, as it focuses the conversation on trade-offs rather than poking holes in a single idea.  
* **Adjusting Projections:** When told to build a low-impact feature by an executive, a product manager should show how that work shifts the impact projections for the established business goals. This moves the conversation from personal disagreement to a shared understanding of business risk.

The landscape of product development is undergoing a fundamental shift from static artifacts to living organisms that continuously learn and adapt through interaction loops. As the marginal cost of high quality output approaches zero, society is entering an agentic era where software agents, both embedded and embodied, drive exponential productivity. This transition necessitates a reconfiguration of organizational structures, where traditional hierarchy is replaced by task oriented work charts. To succeed, companies must prioritize post-training and reinforcement learning over massive pre-training, empower full-stack polymath builders who operate across functional lanes, and focus on the fundamental platform pillars of reliability, privacy, and performance.

## **From Product as Artifact to Product as Organism**

Traditionally, software products were treated as static artifacts, shipped to solve a problem with occasional updates and dashboard monitoring. In the AI era, products have evolved into organisms characterized by a metabolism that allows them to ingest data, digest rewards models, and improve dynamically.

* **Continuous Improvement:** Unlike static software, these products think, live, and learn, becoming more effective with every interaction.  
* **The Metabolism of Product Teams:** The key performance indicator for modern teams is their ability to rapidly iterate through feedback loops to tune models for specific outcomes like price, performance, or quality.  
* **Proprietary Intellectual Property:** This continuous loop of data ingestion and model tuning represents the new intellectual property for every company.

"All of a sudden these are these living organisms that just get better with the more interactions that happen. I think this is the new IP of every single company products that think and live and learn", [Asha Sharma](https://www.linkedin.com/in/aboutasha)

## **The Rise of the Agentic Society and the Work Chart**

The emergence of an agentic society is driven by the decreasing marginal cost of good output. As agents become capable of taking on an exponential number of tasks, the way work is organized must change.

### **The Shift from Org Chart to Work Chart**

In a traditional organization, hierarchy and reporting lines define the structure. In an agentic workforce, the focus shifts to task-based throughput.

* **Task Routing:** Organizations will move toward automatically deciding how to route tasks, observing agent performance, and fine-tuning those agents in real time.  
* **Layer Reduction:** The capability of agents reduces the need for multiple layers of management, as tasks and throughput become the primary metrics of success.  
* **Skill Expansion:** AI agents allow employees to expand their skill sets, essentially bringing their own agent stack to work to increase their baseline capabilities.

"When all of that happens the org chart starts to become the work chart you just don't need as many layers."

### **Embedded vs. Embodied Agents**

* **Embedded Agents:** These function as tools within existing software pieces.  
* **Embodied Agents:** These take on more autonomous roles, such as software development representatives or agents capable of managing pull requests in **GitHub**.

## **The Evolution of Builders and Interfaces**

The advent of AI has historically changed roles, just as the move from mainframes to PCs or cloud to mobile did. The current era favors the polymath, a full-stack builder who can operate across disciplines.

### **The Full-Stack Builder**

Current organizational structures often involve too many touchpoints, such as separate functions for security, user research, and design, which slows down velocity.

* **Converging Disciplines:** The traditional boundaries between product management, engineering, and design are blurring.  
* **Velocity and Throughput:** AI-native companies succeed by employing individuals who can handle the entire loop of product creation rather than staying in a specific functional lane.

"I really believe in the concept of a full-stack builder, you're seeing it with a bunch of the AI native companies that are coming up, i'm even seeing it in enterprises that have been around for 50 years, starting to operate in that way and I think that gives you velocity and throughput and then gives you the whole loop to start to actually metabolize and go through that much faster", [Asha Sharma](https://www.linkedin.com/in/aboutasha)

### **GUI to Code-Native Interfaces**

There is a notable shift from graphical user interfaces (GUIs) to code-native or text-stream interfaces.

* **Composability over Canvas:** Product makers must move away from focusing solely on the visual UI and instead consider how a product composes and how agents read it.  
* **Text-Stream Efficiency:** LLMs interact more effectively with text streams, leading to the success of terminal like environments as seen in tools like **Cursor**.

## **Technical Strategy: The Shift to Post-Training**

As models grow in size, the economic and practical focus of AI development is shifting from pre-training to post-training.

* **The 30 Billion Parameter Threshold:** Once a model exceeds 30 billion parameters, the capital expenditure required for pre-training often becomes less economically viable than optimizing through loops.  
* **Reinforcement Learning (RL):** Post-training, including reinforcement learning and fine-tuning, is becoming the primary method for steering models toward specific use cases.  
* **Model Ensembles:** Rather than relying on a single model to rule them all, successful products often use an ensemble of models, selecting different models based on latency, thinking time, or domain expertise.

"I believe we will see just as much money spent on post-training as we will on pre-training and in the future more on post-training", [Asha Sharma](https://www.linkedin.com/in/aboutasha)

## **Strategic Planning and Seasonal Roadmaps**

Traditional planning cycles, such as six-month semesters, are often too slow for the current pace of AI development. A more effective approach involves planning by seasons.

| Planning Layer | Duration/Focus |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Seasons** | Variable (3-12 months); defined by secular industry changes or customer shifts. |
| **Quarterly OKRs** | Loose goals that align the team with the current season's ethos. |
| **Squad Goals** | 4-6 week sprints focusing on specific problem areas. |

The current season is identified as “the Rise of Agents”. Planning requires leaving slack in the system to account for the slope of technological disruption rather than just a snapshot of the current state.

## **Platform Fundamentals and Leadership**

Success for platforms like those provided by **Microsoft**, **Instacart**, and **Meta** often depends on invisible infrastructure rather than surface-level features.

### **Pillars of Platform Success**

Evidence from successful companies suggests that core infrastructure is the ultimate differentiator:

* **WhatsApp:** Won through reliability, speed, end-to-end encryption, and the simplicity of the phone book rather than stickers or stories.  
* **Instacart:** Depends on an engine that updates a billion items 3,000 times per minute to ensure inventory accuracy.  
* **Porch Group:** Achieved its initial valuation by optimizing a matching engine between professionals and service types across tens of thousands of zip codes.

### **Leadership and Culture**

Leadership in high-stakes environments like **Microsoft** requires generating energy and clarity.

* **Optimism as a Resource:** Effective leadership involves using optimism to renew commitment to a mission every day.  
* **Growth Mindset:** Cultivating a culture that is not afraid of AI but instead uses it to raise the ceiling of what is possible.  
* **Customer-Centricity:** Adopting models like the inverted pyramid at **The Home Depot**, where associates and stores are at the top and headquarters serves as support, ensures technology remains grounded in real-world problems.

"I've learned that optimism is a renewable resource", [Asha Sharma](https://www.linkedin.com/in/aboutasha)

# **The Ultimate Guide to Answer Engine Optimization (AEO)**

## **Executive Summary**

Answer Engine Optimization (AEO), also referred to as Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), represents the most significant shift in digital marketing since the introduction of anti-spam algorithms by **Google** in the late 2000s. As users transition from traditional search engines to Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and **Perplexity**, the mechanics of visibility are fundamentally changing. Unlike traditional SEO, which prioritizes the first blue link, AEO success depends on the frequency and quality of mentions across a variety of citations.

Key insights from the analysis include:

* **High Conversion Value:** Traffic from LLMs can convert at a rate six times higher than traditional search traffic due to high user intent and the conversational nature of the interactions.  
* **Low Barrier for Startups:** Unlike traditional search, which requires months or years to build domain authority, AEO allows new companies to see immediate impact by securing citations in real time.  
* **Citation Dominance:** Winning the answer requires being the most cited source within the RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) process of the model.  
* **The Long-Tail Expansion:** The conversational nature of LLMs creates a much larger tail of specific, complex questions that were previously non-existent in traditional search.  
* **Strategic Diversification:** Optimization must occur both on-site (landing pages) and off-site (**YouTube**, **Reddit**, and high authority affiliates).

## **Understanding AEO and the Current Search Transition**

AEO is essentially SEO for the AI driven world. It is the practice of getting a product or brand to appear as the definitive answer in responses generated by LLMs. [Ethan Smith](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ethanls), CEO of **Graphite**, identifies this as the second largest change in the history of search optimization.

### **Defining AEO and GEO**

While AEO and GEO are often used interchangeably, AEO is the preferred term because it is more narrowly focused on providing specific answers rather than the broader generation of images or videos. The definition of these terms is currently being shaped by community agreement.

### **The Inflection Point**

A major shift occurred in January 2024\. This period saw a significant increase in LLM adoption and a technical evolution in how answers are presented. "The clickability of the answers increased and then the adoption increased and that was around January." Modern LLMs now include maps, shopping carousels, and clickable cards, making them viable referral engines. For example, ChatGPT currently drives more traffic to some major newsletters than **Twitter**.

## **The Mechanics of LLM Responses**

To optimize for AI, one must understand the difference between the core model and the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) layer.

* **Core Model:** This is the foundational algorithm trained on billions of pages. It uses next-word prediction to answer static facts. Influencing the core model is extremely difficult and may take a year to see results.  
* **RAG:** This is a post-training process where the model performs a real-time search and summarizes the results. "I'm mostly focused on the right side because that's the main thing that's controllable."

### **Differences in Results**

Research indicates that citations in LLMs do not perfectly mirror **Google** search results.

* ChatGPT citation overlap with **Google** is approximately 35%.  
* **Perplexity** citation overlap with **Google** is approximately 70%.  
* Different surfaces (e.g., Gemini vs. **Meta**) will provide different answers based on their specific search configurations.

## **Strategic Framework for AEO Success**

Success in AEO requires a tactical, seven-step approach that balances traditional search techniques with new, citation-based strategies.

### **1\. Question Research**

The first step is identifying the specific questions users are asking.

* Transform existing search data and paid search terms into questions.

# **The Strategic Importance of AI Evaluations in Product Development**

## **Executive Summary**

AI evaluations, commonly referred to as evals, have emerged as a critical skill for product builders. They represent a systematic approach to measuring and improving AI applications through data analytics, moving beyond informal vibe checks to rigorous, actionable metrics. The development of an effective eval suite is described as a high ROI activity that enables teams to iterate with confidence. Key takeaways from industry experts at **Anthropic** and **OpenAI** suggest that evals are becoming the most important new skill for successful AI product managers and engineers.

The process begins with manual error analysis and open coding, where practitioners review conversation traces to identify failure modes. One individual, typically the product manager acting as a benevolent dictator, should define the standard of quality to ensure the process remains tractable. These manual notes are then synthesized into axial codes, categories of failure modes, which can be counted to prioritize improvements. Automated evaluators, including code based tests and LLM as judge prompts, are then implemented to monitor production and prevent regressions. Ultimately, evals serve as a living version of a Product Requirements Document, defining exact behaviors and expectations for AI agents in a way that is both automatic and continuous.

## **Defining AI Evaluations**

Evals are a way to systematically measure and improve an AI application. They are essentially data analytics applied to Large Language Model (LLM) applications to provide a feedback signal for iteration. While often compared to unit tests in software engineering, evals cover a broader spectrum of application quality, addressing both non-negotiable functionalities and vague or ambiguous user interactions.

### **The Value of Evals**

* Actionable Improvement: The primary objective is not to achieve perfection but to actionably improve the product based on real-world performance.  
* Systematic Measurement: Evals replace guessing and reliance on luck when adjusting prompts, providing confidence that changes do not break existing functionality.  
* Highest ROI: Investing in evals is considered the highest return on investment activity for AI product builders.

## **The Error Analysis Framework**

The foundation of an effective eval system is error analysis, a technique used in machine learning for decades to analyze stochastic systems. This involves a deep dive into logs or traces, which are sequences of events representing a single interaction between a user and the AI.

### **Steps in Initial Error Analysis**

1. Review Traces: Examine detailed logs, including system prompts, tool calls, and retrieval data, to understand the context of the interaction.  
2. Open Coding: Write detailed, informal notes on what went wrong in a specific trace. It is essential to capture the most upstream error first.  
3. Theoretical Saturation: Continue reviewing traces until no new types of errors or concepts are uncovered. This often occurs after reviewing 40 to 100 traces.

### **The Role of the Benevolent Dictator**

To keep the process efficient, teams should avoid decision making by committee. Instead, they should appoint a benevolent dictator, usually a person with domain expertise like a product manager, whose taste and judgment define the standard for what constitutes an error. This ensures the evaluation process is affordable and actionable.

## **Categorization: From Open Coding to Axial Coding**

Once a sufficient number of traces have been annotated with open codes, the next step is to synthesize these notes into failure modes known as axial codes.

### **Synthesis Process**

| Stage | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| Open Coding | Raw, detailed notes about specific errors (e.g., did not confirm call transfer). |
| Axial Coding | Categorizing open codes into broader, actionable failure modes (e.g., Human Handoff Issues). |
| Counting | Creating a pivot table to quantify the prevalence of each failure mode to prioritize fixes. |

LLMs are highly effective at this stage of the process. A practitioner can provide a list of open codes to an AI, such as **Google** Gemini or **Anthropic** Claude, and ask it to generate axial codes. These categories must be specific and actionable, such as formatting errors or promising follow-ups that were not kept.

## **Building Automated Evaluators**

After identifying and prioritizing failure modes, teams develop automated evaluators to monitor these issues at scale.

### **Code-Based Evaluators**

Whenever possible, teams should use code-based evals because they are cheaper and deterministic. These are suitable for checking objective criteria such as:

* Output length or specific string presence.  
* JSON or Markdown formatting.  
* Successful tool calls or database queries.

### **LLM as Judge**

For subjective or complex behaviors, an LLM can be used as a judge. This involves a tightly scoped prompt that asks the LLM to evaluate a single, specific failure mode.

* Binary Scoring: Judges should output a simple true or false or pass or fail result. Numerical scales (e.g., 1 to 5\) are often misleading and difficult to act upon.  
* Alignment: Before deploying an LLM judge, its performance must be measured against human judgment. If the judge and the human expert disagree, the judge prompt must be iterated upon until high agreement is reached.  
* Production Monitoring: These judges can be used for online monitoring, sampling production traces daily to track failure rates in real time.

## **Addressing Industry Misconceptions and Debates**

There is significant controversy in the AI community regarding the necessity of formal evals versus vibe checks.

### **Common Misconceptions**

* Complete Automation: A major pitfall is attempting to use an LLM to perform initial error analysis. "I think in these cases it's so important to make sure you are manually doing this yourself." LLMs lack the product context and domain expertise to identify subtle product smell or specific business hallucinations.  
* Vibe Checks: Some developers claim they only use vibes to evaluate products. However, expert teams dogfooding their own products are often performing implicit, high speed error analysis. For most domains, such as legal or medical applications, this informal approach is insufficient and dangerous.

### **Evals as the New PRD**

In the context of AI development, evals effectively function as the modern Product Requirements Document (PRD). They explicitly define how a product should behave across a multitude of specific scenarios. Unlike a traditional static document, an eval suite is a running, automated system that ensures the product continues to meet requirements as it evolves. "You don't really know what you want until you see it with these LLMs."

## **Conclusion for Product Builders**

Implementing a structured eval process is a necessary evolution for AI product development. By grounding evaluations in real data through manual error analysis, then scaling those insights via axial coding and automated judges, teams can transform the chaotic behavior of LLMs into a reliable, high-quality user experience. Companies like **Anthropic**, **OpenAI**, **Nurture Boss**, and **Synthesia** utilize these systematic approaches to maintain their competitive edge. For product managers, mastering evals is the most effective way to ensure their AI agents deliver accurate, helpful, and brand-aligned results consistently.

# **Nesrine Changuel’s Strategic Framework for Product Delight**

## **Executive Summary**

Product delight is a measurable business strategy that combines functional utility with emotional connection to drive user retention, loyalty, and market differentiation. While many product teams view delight as superficial aesthetic additions, referred to as confetti, it is more accurately defined as the intersection of joy and surprise. To build delightful products systematically, teams must move beyond demographic segmentation to identify motivational drivers, categorizing them as both functional and emotional. This document outlines a four step framework for discovering high ROI opportunities, prioritizes features using a 50/40/10 distribution model, and provides a validation checklist to ensure features are inclusive and aligned with business goals.

## **Defining Product Delight**

Delight is not a luxury or a decorative layer, it is a competitive differentiator in crowded markets. It is defined through two lenses, the theoretical and the practical.

* **Theoretical Definition:** Based on the wheel of emotion, delight is a combination of joy and surprise. "Delight is actually this ability to create products that serve for both emotional need and functional need."  
* **Practical Definition:** Practical delight involves creating an experience where emotion is at the core of the utility. "Delight is not about sprinkling joy on top of utility, it's about creating an experience where emotion is completely on the heart of the experience."

## **The Three Pillars of Delightful Experiences**

Building delight requires satisfying three specific pillars of product design.

| Pillar | Description | Example |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Removing Friction** | Identifying valley moments where user emotion is low and removing stress. | **Uber** allowing a refund in two clicks after a driver cancellation. |
| **Anticipating Needs** | Offering a solution before the user explicitly asks for it. | **Revolute** offering an integrated eSIM feature for international travelers. |
| **Exceeding Expectations** | Providing more value than the user expected from the interaction. | **Microsoft** Edge automatically finding and applying a 15 percent discount coupon at checkout. |

## **The Business to Human (B2H) Approach**

The distinction between B2B and B2C is increasingly irrelevant regarding delight, as all products are ultimately used by humans with emotional needs. This concept, termed B2H, emphasizes that even enterprise companies like **Salesforce**, **Workday**, and **SAP** can improve retention by honoring human emotion.

* **Humanization:** A type of delighter that treats the product as a human agent. For instance, **Google** Meet developers compared the video conferencing experience to a real conversation rather than a competitor's software.  
* **Trust and Integrity:** Delight can manifest as transparency. **Buffer** reached out to inactive users to offer refunds, which prioritized trust over immediate revenue and built a deeper emotional connection.  
* **Standout Quality:** In a green field market, solving a pain point is sufficient, but in competitive markets like task management, companies like **Linear** win by being more thoughtful about the experience.

## **The Four Step Delight Model**

This repeatable approach helps teams prioritize features that drive KPIs rather than low impact confetti.

### **Step 1: Identify User Motivators**

Teams should segment users based on why they use the product rather than just who they are.

* **Functional Motivators:** Practical tasks, such as finding a specific track on **Spotify**.  
* **Personal Emotional Motivators:** How the user wants to feel while using the product, such as feeling secure or productive.  
* **Social Emotional Motivators:** How the user wants others to perceive them, such as feeling cool by sharing a **Spotify** Wrapped summary.

### **Step 2: Convert Motivators to Opportunities**

Transform identified motivators into strategic product opportunities. This involves shifting from a problem space to an opportunity space where solutions honor needs.

### **Step 3: Identify and Categorize Solutions**

Use the Delight Grid to categorize potential features into three types:

* **Surface Delight:** Solves only an emotional motivator, such as the birthday balloons on an **Apple** Watch.  
* **Low Delight:** Solves only a functional motivator, ensuring the product works as intended.  
* **Deep Delight:** The most powerful category, solving both a functional and emotional need simultaneously. **Spotify** Discover Weekly is an example, as it provides music, functional, while making the user feel seen and understood, emotional.

### **Step 4: Validate via the Delight Checklist**

Before implementation, every feature must pass a checklist to ensure it adds genuine value.

* **User and Business Impact:** Does it align with core KPIs?  
* **Feasibility and Familiarity:** Is the innovation too shocking? Users appreciate a degree of familiarity, as seen when **Spotify** users preferred a version of Discover Weekly that accidentally included some already liked songs.  
* **Inclusion:** Features must be checked for potential negative emotional impacts. For example, an **Apple** feature that triggered fireworks during a sensitive therapy session via hand gestures was not inclusive of all contexts.

## **Strategy and Prioritization: The 50/40/10 Rule**

To maintain a balanced roadmap, teams should avoid a binary choice between functionality and delight. Instead, they should integrate delight into functionality using a specific distribution:

* **50 Percent Low Delight:** Focus on core functionality to ensure the product is reliable and useful.  
* **40 Percent Deep Delight:** Features that blend utility with emotional connection.  
* **10 Percent Surface Delight:** Small, joyful elements that build brand personality and a sense of celebration, like the **Airbnb** Superhost confetti.

Implementing a delight culture has benefits beyond user metrics.

* **Employee Motivation:** Working on delightful features increases PM and engineering motivation because they witness direct user love and positive reactions.  
* **Avoiding Habituation:** Surprise vanishes over time. "It's about creating that surprise over time in a complete continuous way to avoid that habituation effect." This requires a continuous plan for innovation, such as **Google** Meet evolving from simple background blurs to AI generated immersive environments.  
* **Rituals:** Organizations can foster this by creating delight days or hack weeks, as seen at **Migros** and **Spotify**, to encourage out of the box thinking and playful innovation.

# **Nicole Forsgren on AI Developer Productivity and Experience**

## **Executive Summary**

The integration of artificial intelligence into software engineering necessitates a fundamental shift in how organizations measure and manage developer productivity. While AI tools accelerate the initial generation of code, they also introduce new bottlenecks, including increased technical debt, reliability concerns, and a significant shift from writing code to reviewing it. Traditional metrics, particularly lines of code, are increasingly irrelevant and susceptible to system gaming. To achieve sustainable performance gains, companies must prioritize Developer Experience, which focuses on reducing friction, managing cognitive load, and optimizing feedback loops. Success in this emerging landscape requires a product-led approach to engineering processes, emphasizing qualitative insights from developers alongside quantitative business outcomes like velocity and market share.

## **Defining Developer Experience**

Developer Experience refers to the day-to-day reality of building software within an organization. It encompasses the workflows, tools, and support systems that either facilitate or hinder an engineer's ability to deliver value. High quality Developer Experience is critical because it directly influences engineering happiness and organizational success.

Key components of Developer Experience include:

* Flow State: The ability for an engineer to enter a focused, productive state of deep work without unnecessary interruptions.  
* Cognitive Load: The mental effort required to navigate complex systems or mechanics, which, if too high, leaves little room for innovation.  
* Feedback Loops: The speed and quality of responses an engineer receives from systems, such as build results or customer feedback.

## **The Impact of AI on Engineering Workflows**

AI is fundamentally altering how engineers structure their work and manage their attention. While it can act as a partner in unblocking tasks and providing context, it also introduces significant challenges regarding trust and oversight.

* Shift to Reviewing: A primary shift in the engineering role is the transition from authoring code to evaluating it. "So much of the time is now going to be spent reviewing code versus writing code"  
* Trust and Reliability: Large language models are non-deterministic, meaning their output requires rigorous evaluation for hallucinations, style consistency, and reliability. "One of the biggest issues we're going to probably have with AI is learning how much to trust code that it generates"  
* Interruption-Driven Development: AI can create an environment full of interruptions, as the machine interjects during the coding process. However, AI can also help engineers regain flow by summarizing context or generating system diagrams.  
* Deep Work Constraints: Human attention for deep work is limited to approximately four hours per day. AI tools may enable engineers to make shorter work blocks, such as 45-minute sessions, more useful by handling the initial setup of tasks.

## **The Failure of Traditional Productivity Metrics**

Conventional methods of measuring engineering output are insufficient in the AI era. Relying on simple volume based metrics leads to misleading conclusions and can harm the technical health of a codebase.

* Lines of Code: This metric is now easily manipulated by verbose AI agents and does not reflect complexity or value. "Most productivity metrics are a lie if the goal is more lines of code I can prompt something to write the longest piece of code ever it's just too easy to gain that system"  
* Velocity vs. Quality: Speed without strategy results in wasted effort. "We can ship trash faster every single day we need strategy and really smart decisions to know what to ship"  
* Framework Adaptation: Established frameworks like DORA and SPACE must be adapted. DORA, which focuses on deployment frequency, lead time, change fail rate, and time to restore, remains useful for assessing the overall pipeline but misses early-stage feedback loops facilitated by AI.

The SPACE framework provides a more holistic view by examining:

| Dimension | Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| Satisfaction | The well-being and fulfillment of the developers. |
| Performance | The overall outcome and business impact. |
| Activity | Countable actions like pull requests or alerts, used with caution. |
| Communication | How systems and people collaborate, including offloading to chatbots. |
| Efficiency and Flow | The ability to complete work with minimal friction and high focus. |

## **The Frictionless Framework for Organizational Improvement**

To remove barriers and improve performance, organizations can follow a seven-step process developed by [Nicole Forsgren](https://www.linkedin.com/in/nicolefv) and [Abi Noda](https://www.linkedin.com/in/abinoda) of **DX**, which was recently acquired by **Atlassian**.

1. Start the Journey: Conduct a listening tour with developers to identify friction points and visualize current workflows.  
2. Get a Quick Win: Identify and complete small, high-impact projects to build momentum.  
3. Use Data to Optimize: Establish a data foundation using surveys and existing telemetry to find optimization opportunities.  
4. Decide Strategy and Priority: Evaluate identified issues and prioritize them based on their impact on the organization.  
5. Sell Your Strategy: Communicate the rationale to stakeholders to gain necessary feedback and alignment.  
6. Drive Change at Scale: Implement changes across the organization, leveraging both grassroots efforts and top-down leadership support.  
7. Evaluate Progress and Show Value: Continuously measure the impact of improvements and loop back to the start of the process.

## **Measuring Business Value and Return on Investment**

Demonstrating the value of Developer Experience initiatives requires aligning engineering metrics with broader business goals. Leaders at companies like **Google**, **Airbnb**, and **Mercury** often prioritize different outcomes based on their strategic focus.

* Market Share and Competitiveness: If leadership focuses on growth, metrics should emphasize speed, specifically the time from feature idea to experiment or customer feedback.  
* Profit Margins: When cost is the primary concern, focus on quantifying savings from reduced cloud costs, consolidated vendor spend, or recovered developer capacity.  
* Qualitative Baselines: Surveys are essential for capturing sentiment on tools and processes. They should ask developers to prioritize their top three barriers and specify the frequency of these issues to create a weighted score of friction.  
* Attribution: When reporting gains, it is necessary to disclose that improvements likely result from the combination of AI tools and Developer Experience enhancements, rather than a single factor alone.

* The risk of overcommitting to a technology that may leave a company stuck.

## **Technical Foundations: Training and Language Modeling**

Understanding the mechanics of how models are built is essential for engineering effective products.

### **Pre-training and Post-training**

Pre-training involves encoding statistical information from massive datasets, such as the entire internet, to predict the next token. Tokens represent a middle ground between characters and words, helping to reduce vocabulary size while maintaining meaning.

"language modeling is a way of encoding statistical information about a language"

While pre-training increases general capabilities, post-training is where the most significant energy is currently spent. This phase includes:

* **Supervised Fine-tuning:** Using demonstration data from human experts to emulate high-quality responses.  
* **Distillation:** Training smaller models to emulate the outputs of larger, more successful models.  
* **Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF):** Using human comparisons to train a reward model, which then scores and encourages better outputs from the AI.

### **Verifiable Rewards and Domain Expertise**

Reinforcement learning is expanding into verifiable rewards, such as math or physics problems where the correctness of an answer is objectively measurable. This creates a high demand for high quality, labeled data provided by specialized startups. This economic landscape is currently lopsided, as a few frontier labs serve as the primary customers for a large number of data providers.

## **Evaluation Frameworks and ROI**

### **The Purpose of Evals**

Evals serve to guide product development and uncover opportunities for improvement. They can be divided into:

* **App-specific Evals:** Measuring if a specific tool, like a chatbot, is meeting user needs.  
* **Task-specific Evals:** Measuring specific capabilities, such as creative writing or code generation.

### **The Question of Investment**

Building comprehensive evals requires significant engineering resources. For many features, a vive check or simple observation of user traffic may be sufficient. However, tyrannical focus on metrics is required when:

* The application operates at a scale where failure has catastrophic consequences.  
* High visibility into model performance is necessary to understand failure modes.  
* The feature serves as a core competitive advantage.

## **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and Data Preparation**

RAG provides models with relevant context from external sources to improve answer quality. While many practitioners agonize over which vector database to use, the most significant performance gains come from data preparation.

### **Effective Data Processing Strategies**

"data preparation is just like hands out" Superior RAG performance is driven by how data is structured before retrieval:

* **Chunking:** Determining the optimal size for data segments to ensure they contain enough context without being overly long.  
* **Metadata and Hypothetical Questions:** Adding summaries or generating questions that a chunk can answer to improve retrieval accuracy.  
* **Format Rewriting:** Converting documentation or podcast transcripts into question-and-answer formats, making them more accessible for AI reading compared to human reading.  
* **Annotation Layers:** Adding explanations for scales or abstract terms that an AI might not understand through common sense alone.

# **Jen Abel on Scaling Enterprise Sales from $1M to $10M ARR**

## **Executive Summary**

Transitioning from founder-led sales to a structured enterprise sales organization requires a fundamental shift in market perception, pricing strategy, and hiring practices. The core thesis for scaling to $10M ARR centers on the rejection of the mid-market as a viable segment, prioritizing instead Tier 1 logos as early adopters. Successful enterprise sales at this stage rely on vision casting, selling opportunity and alpha rather than technical problem-solving. Scaling the sales team involves finding individuals who can perform founder cosplay, blending deep product expertise with the ability to navigate complex corporate relationships without relying on automated, high-volume outreach tools.

## **Market Segmentation and Targeting Strategy**

### **The Myth of the Mid-Market**

The mid-market is often perceived as a safe middle ground between small businesses and large enterprises, yet it lacks a distinct definition or approach. Selling to a 100 person organization is a fundamentally different game than selling to a 1,000-person organization.

* **Distinct Silos:** Sales strategies should be divided strictly into small business, which is marketing led, and enterprise, which is sales led.  
* **The Power Law of Corporations:** Power laws exist in large corporations, with the **Fortune 1000** holding the vast majority of weight and value.  
* **Strategic Focus:** Attempting a hybrid approach that bleeds these two games leads to failure. Companies must decide if they are targeting the upper end of small business or the lower end of enterprise and hire accordingly.

### **Tier 1 Logos as Early Adopters**

Counter to traditional venture capital advice, startups should target Tier 1 logos such as **Walmart**, **McDonald's**, **Nvidia**, **Tesla**, **Exxon Mobile**, and **United Healthcare** early in their journey.

* **The Alpha Incentive:** Industry leaders take risks to maintain their number one spot. Providing even a slight bit of alpha can lead to internal promotions for the buyer and a pat on the back for the executive.  
* **Roadmap Influence:** These companies want to dictate product roadmaps. Their involvement can rapidly transform a $100,000 deal into a million-dollar deal.  
* **Referenceability:** Winning the market leader creates a domino effect where the second, third, and fourth-place competitors follow suit.

## **Strategic Positioning: Vision Casting vs. Problem Selling**

The most effective enterprise sales are driven by vision rather than technical scripts. This approach is particularly effective for founders who naturally lean toward opportunity based narratives.

| Aspect | Problem Selling | Vision Casting |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Focus** | Highly specific and technical. | Opportunity and long term alpha. |
| **Audience** | Junior or technical managers. | Senior executives and leaders. |
| **Goal** | Solve a current pain point. | Transform the organization into a superhero. |
| **Vibe** | Feels like a sales script. | Feels like a strategic partnership. |

* **Selling the Gap:** "When you're selling to a leader you need to be selling an opportunity"  
* **Creating Superheroes:** The goal is to sell the buyer on what they become because of the product, such as the ability to hire 10x engineers who would not otherwise join the company.  
* **Avoiding Comparison:** Becoming one of three tools being tested in a comparison is often a sign of a lost deal. Differentiation is critical to success.

## **Pricing and Deal Structuring**

### **The $100,000 Standard**

Founders often struggle with insecurity regarding large ACVs, but enterprise buyers are accustomed to initial contracts between $75,000 and $150,000.

* **The Math of Scaling:** For venture-backed companies, selling $10,000 deals in the enterprise sector is a losing strategy. It is easier to grow a $100,000 deal to $500,000 than to grow a $10,000 deal by the same magnitude.  
* **Executive Buy-in:** Higher price points ensure executive sign-off and involvement. A $10,000 deal can be as difficult to pass through procurement as a $100,000 deal, but the latter guarantees the company is committed to making the implementation successful.  
* **Qualification:** If a client attempts to nickel and dime the price, they are likely not fully bought into the value proposition.

### **Leveraging Services and Design Partnerships**

* **The Service Backdoor:** Large enterprises are highly comfortable buying services. Selling a service powered by technology on the backend is the fastest way to gain entry.  
* **Prorated Transitions:** If services are used to enter a company, the costs should be prorated to habituate the client to the eventual software pricing.  
* **Managing Design Partners:** These are the hardest logos to upsell because they were present when the product was janky. Founders must set the framing early, offering concessions in perpetuity for their early help while clearly defining future price points.

Transitioning from founder led sales typically occurs around the $1M ARR mark, once there is pattern recognition from the first seven to ten customers.

* **Founder Cosplay:** The ideal first hire is someone who can act as a proxy for the founder, selling the vision and excitement of the product rather than just following a playbook.  
* **Profile of Success:** Deep product experience or engineering backgrounds can be more effective than traditional sales experience. Former founders also make excellent hires.  
* **The Rule of Two:** Because the failure rate for first sales hires is approximately 50%, it is recommended to hire two people simultaneously to compare performance.

### **Incentives and Management**

* **Compensation Structures:** Standard technology sales compensation is usually split 50/50 between base salary and on-target earnings (OTE).  
* **Commission:** Commissions typically range between 8% and 12% of the deal size.  
* **Founder Involvement:** The founder should remain involved in the first several calls to ensure the hire has what it takes to navigate the vision.

## **Tactical Execution in Outreach**

### **The Case for Manual Outreach**

In an era of AI driven automation and saturated databases, manual, highly personalized outreach provides the necessary alpha to stand out to executives.

* **The Backdoor Approach:** Avoid the front door where everyone else is trick-or-treating. Finding prospects outside of standard databases and contacting them with personalized, human notes is more effective for high-value deals.  
* **Timing and Context:** Outreach should be driven by emotional cues and external events, such as a company appearing in the news or a specific executive's tenure.  
* **Tone Matching:** The structure of an email should reflect the recipient. A chief legal officer may require a one sentence note sent on a Saturday, while other leads might respond better to different formats.

### **Relationship-Based Closing**

Enterprise deals are closed through trust and individual relationships rather than formal systems.

* **Text Message Closing:** Many successful enterprise deals are finalized over text messaging, signifying a high level of personal trust.  
* **Co-authoring Deals:** Working with the buyer to structure the deal ensures that when they go to bat for the product internally, it is a guaranteed win for them.  
* **Hard Questions:** "Ask the questions you're afraid to, cuz that truth is going to get you closer and closer to the answer." This includes asking if a deal is actually possible within the current year.

# **Stewart Butterfield on Product Development and Organizational Leadership**

## **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes the core themes, product philosophies, and organizational management strategies discussed by [Stewart Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield), the founder of **Flickr** and **Slack**, in a detailed analysis of his career and leadership principles.

The following insights represent the critical takeaways for product leaders and organizational executives:

* **The Utility Curve:** Products and features do not provide value linearly. Effort often yields no value until a magic threshold is crossed, followed by a steep increase in value and eventually diminishing returns.  
* **Comprehension over Friction:** The standard industry goal of reducing clicks or friction is often misplaced. The primary challenge is usually comprehension, ensuring the user understands what the product is and what the next action will achieve.  
* **Hyperrealistic Work like Activities:** Mature organizations often engage in activities that superficially resemble work, such as meetings, slide decks, and over analysis of minor features, without creating actual value.  
* **The Owner's Delusion:** Product creators often overstate the importance of their software in the lives of users, ignoring that most users have low intent and limited cognitive resources.  
* **Customer Value as Success:** The long term measure of success is the actual value created for customers, not the perceived value or clever marketing.

## **The Utility Curve of Product Development**

[Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield) defines the utility curve as a familiar S curve where the horizontal axis represents cost or effort and the vertical axis represents value or convenience.

* **The Value Threshold:** Initial effort in creating a database or basic table generates zero value until a specific threshold is reached where the tool becomes functional.  
* **Binary Feature Fallacy:** Features are often incorrectly viewed as binary, you either have them or you do not. The utility curve suggests that if an organization does not invest enough to move up the steep part of the curve, the feature adds complexity without providing value.  
* **Divine Discontent:** Standards for quality and convenience are moving targets. As competitors improve, the expected threshold for value increases, requiring continued investment even in standard features like login or search.  
* **The Importance of Delight:** While users may not switch products solely because of a superior time zone picker, minor conveniences create an emotional connection. This connection leads to organic advocacy when users move between companies.

## **Product Craft and the Philosophy of Courtesy**

[Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield) advocates for product craftsmanship driven by empathy for the user experience, a concept he illustrates with the metaphor of tilting an umbrella.

* **Tilting the Umbrella:** Observing people on narrow sidewalks in the rain, [Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield) noted that many do not tilt their umbrellas to avoid hitting others. In product terms, failing to exercise this courtesy is an opportunity for competitors to create a critical advantage.  
* **Craft as Advantage:** Most people and companies do not have good taste or invest in craft. By being considerate and empathic about the user's experience, a product can facilitate growth through cross pollination and genuine user advocacy.  
* **The Shouty Rooster:** To manage the tragedy of the commons in **Slack**, the team implemented a notification warning. "Hey this is going to cause a notification for 147 people in eight different time zones. Are you sure you want to send this message with the at everyone?" This feature shaped communication culture by teaching users the cost of their actions.  
* **Strategic Defaults:** In the rollout of the “Do Not Disturb” feature, **Slack** utilized a complex system of defaults. While users could override them, the presence of a default ensured the feature was utilized by the average person who otherwise would not seek it out.

A significant portion of the discussion focuses on the psychological and biological costs of using software.

* **The Glucose Cost of Thinking:** Making a decision burns glucose and causes decision fatigue. If software forces a user to think without providing clarity, it creates an emotional association of feeling stupid.  
* **Intent and Specificity:** High intent users, such as those buying [Taylor Swift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_Swift) tickets on **Ticketmaster**, will persist through friction. Most users of new software, however, have intent just above the minimum threshold. For these users, the challenge is not friction but comprehension: what is this, and what do I do next?  
* **The Error of Click Reduction:** Reducing the number of clicks is the wrong goal if it increases the cognitive load of each step. [Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield) notes that teenage users of **Snapchat** may tap several times per second with high fluidity because each action is a simple, non-fraught decision.

[Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield) applies Parkinson’s Law to explain how organizations become bloated and inefficient as they scale.

### **Parkinson’s Law and Hiring**

The law states that work expands to fill the time available for its completion. In an organizational context, this manifests as a self perpetuating cycle of hiring.

* **Managerial Incentives:** Most employees want to hire more people because the number of direct reports correlates with career trajectory, pay, and authority.  
* **Butterfield's Law:** "Everything is simple if you have no idea what you're talking about." This suggests that if a problem seems simple, the observer likely lacks a full understanding of its complexity.

### **Hyperrealistic Work-like Activities**

As an organization matures, the supply of known valuable work decreases relative to the number of employees. This leads to hyperrealistic work, activities that are superficially identical to work but produce no value.

* **The Regression Example:** [Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield) cites an instance where **Slack** spent thousands of person hours on a minor feature regression involving tags in message threads. The cost of the meetings, analytics, and A/B testing far outweighed any possible utility the feature could provide.

* **Smart Folding:** Using concepts from game theory, a pivot is a smart fold, a decision made when the expected value of the current path has diminished to the point where an alternative is more attractive.  
* **The Owner's Delusion:** This concept explains why many products fail. Owners assume users care about their product as much as they do, leading to slow-loading websites with music and inscrutable buttons.

The document concludes with [Butterfield](https://www.linkedin.com/in/butterfield)’s philosophy on the ethical and practical necessity of creating customer value.

The security challenges facing AI systems primarily manifest through two distinct but related methods of manipulation.

### **Jailbreaking**

Jailbreaking occurs in a direct interaction between a user and a model. The user employs complex or deceptive prompts to bypass the safety training of the model to elicit prohibited information. "Jailbreaking is like when it is just you and the model." Examples include tricking a system into providing instructions for illegal acts or creating dangerous substances.

### **Prompt Injection**

Prompt injection is more complex, involving a third party or a developer defined system prompt. In this scenario, a malicious user provides input that instructs the model to ignore its original developer instructions in favor of the user's new, malicious commands. "Prompt injection occurs when somebody has like built an application, or like sometimes an agent depending on the situation."

| Attack Type | Components Involved | Core Objective |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Jailbreaking | User \+ AI Model | Bypass safety filters to get prohibited outputs. |
| Prompt Injection | User \+ AI Model \+ System Prompt | Override developer instructions to hijack application logic. |
| Indirect Prompt Injection | User \+ Data Source \+ AI Agent | Use external data (like emails or websites) to trick an agent. |

## **The Fallacy of AI Guardrails and Automated Red Teaming**

The AI security industry currently promotes two primary solutions that research suggests are fundamentally flawed: automated red teaming and AI guardrails.

### **Ineffectiveness of Guardrails**

Guardrails are additional models placed in front of or behind a primary model to classify inputs and outputs as valid or malicious. These systems fail for several reasons:

* **Infinite Attack Space:** For advanced models like GPT5, the number of possible prompts is essentially infinite, making it statistically impossible for a guardrail to catch every permutation. "The number of possible attacks against another LLM is equivalent to the number of possible prompts."  
* **Human Adaptability:** In controlled competitions, human attackers break 100% of state-of-the-art defenses within 10 to 30 attempts.  
* **Brain vs. Software:** Unlike traditional software where a security flaw can be permanently fixed, the probabilistic nature of AI makes it impossible to guarantee a specific behavior is truly patched. "You can patch a bug but you can't patch a brain."

### **Misleading Security Metrics**

Many security companies claim high effectiveness rates, such as 99% adversarial robustness. However, these statistics are often considered fabricated or based on non-statistically significant data sets. "When these guardrail providers say we catch everything that is a complete lie." Furthermore, these systems often fail to account for non-English languages, allowing attackers to bypass defenses simply by translating their malicious prompts.

## **The Evolution of Risk: From Chatbots to Agents**

The danger posed by insecure AI is increasing as systems move from passive information retrieval to active agency.

### **Agentic Vulnerabilities**

Agents are AI systems capable of taking actions on behalf of a user, such as reading and sending emails or interacting with company databases. Recent incidents involving **ServiceNow** demonstrated that a benign agent could be manipulated into recruiting more powerful agents to perform unauthorized database actions and exfiltrate information.

### **Robotics and Physical Safety**

The integration of visual language models into robotics introduces physical risks. If a robot is controlled by a prompt, injectable model, a bystander could potentially voice a command that tricks the robot into causing physical injury. "We have already seen people jailbreaking LM powered robotic systems."

### **The Capabilities Gap**

Currently, the lack of massive, damaging attacks is attributed to early adoption and limited model intelligence rather than robust security. As models become more capable, their ability to execute complex, malicious tasks will increase. Frontier labs, including **OpenAI**, **Anthropic**, and **Google**, are incentivized to prioritize model intelligence and speed over security to remain competitive in the market.

## **Strategic Recommendations for Organizations**

Given that a perfect technical solution for AI security does not exist, organizations should adopt a pragmatic, risk, based approach.

### **1\. Evaluate the Deployment Scope**

If an AI deployment is limited to a read-only chatbot providing information from FAQs or documents, the risk is primarily reputational. In these cases, the user can only hurt themselves, and expensive guardrail systems offer little added value.

### **2\. Implement Rigorous Permissioning**

The most effective defense is limiting the actions an AI can take. Organizations should use frameworks like Camel, which restricts an agent's permissions based on the specific user request.

* If a user asks for a summary of emails, the system should only have read permissions.  
* If a user asks to send an email, it should not have permission to read the inbox. "It really is about getting the permissioning right ahead of time."

### **3\. Integrate Classical Cybersecurity**

AI security should not be treated as a separate silo. Traditional security practices, such as running code in isolated containers (dockerization) and strict data access controls, remain essential. Organizations should hire personnel who understand the intersection of AI research and classical systems security.

### **4\. Continuous Monitoring and Education**

All AI inputs and outputs should be logged and reviewed. This is not for real-time blocking but for understanding how the system is being used and identifying potential vulnerabilities. Education is also vital, as aware developers are less likely to deploy systems that allow an AI to execute unverified code on a primary server.

## **Future Outlook and Market Corrections**

The AI security industry is expected to undergo a market correction as enterprises realize that current guardrail products do not provide the promised security. Revenue for these companies may dry up as more effective, open-source alternatives become available and the limitations of the technology are better understood.

The most promising long-term research involves adversarial training deeper in the model training stack, essentially training the model to be robust from the pre-training phase. However, until such foundational breakthroughs occur, the industry must rely on controlling the environment in which the AI operates. "The only reason there has not been a massive attack yet, is how early the adoption is, not because it is secure."

# **Matt MacInnis on Strategic Leadership and Product Excellence at Rippling**

This briefing document synthesizes key leadership lessons and product management frameworks derived from the experience of [Matt MacInnis](https://www.linkedin.com/in/macinnis), Chief Product Officer and former Chief Operating Officer at **Rippling**. The analysis details the operational philosophy that has supported the growth of **Rippling** to a 16 billion dollar valuation, focusing on the necessity of high intensity, strategic understaffing, and the prioritization of product as the primary driver of business success.

## **Executive Summary**

The core philosophy presented in the source context is that extraordinary results are impossible without extraordinary effort. Management at **Rippling** operates on the principle that the comfort zone is a mistake, and true excellence requires a relentless injection of energy to combat systemic entropy. Key takeaways include:

* Deliberate understaffing is a tool to prevent organizational politics and ensure focus on high-priority tasks.  
* The Alpha and Beta framework distinguishes between outperformance (Alpha) and volatility (Beta), suggesting that processes should minimize volatility without suppressing innovation.  
* Product is the high order bit of a company, getting the product right simplifies all other functions, including sales, finance, and recruiting.  
* Success provides more valuable learning than failure, as partaking in a winning team reveals the mechanics of excellence.  
* The AI landscape favors companies with first-party data (the mine) and the technology (the shovel), while point solutions in the middle face economic challenges.

## **The Necessity of Extraordinary Effort**

A foundational belief at **Rippling** is that achieving outcomes in the 99th percentile requires being in a state of discomfort. High-performance teams are separated from good teams during moments of high stress and exhaustion, such as triaging bugs or escalations on a Friday night.

* Intensity as a Requirement: If employees find themselves in a comfort zone, they are failing to reach the necessary level of effort for world-class outcomes.  
* "It's supposed to be really freaking exhausting."  
* Maintaining Momentum: In a competitive market, any relaxation of effort provides an opening for competitors. Relentlessness is necessary because a hungry competitor will fill any crack left by the organization.  
* Morale and Activity: Counterintuitively, morale often drops when teams have too much free time. Keeping people busy, motivated, and fired up is more effective for maintaining morale than slowing down for breaks.

## **Strategic Operational Frameworks**

Management is viewed as the application of frameworks to help people achieve goals together. Because the exact right answer for budget or staffing is rarely knowable upfront, leaders must choose whether to over-steer or under-steer relative to a perceived midpoint.

### **Deliberate Understaffing**

At **Rippling**, every project is deliberately understaffed. This is considered a less evil alternative to overstaffing for several reasons:

* Prevention of Politics: Overstaffed teams tend to descend into political infighting.  
* Focus on Priorities: When a team is overstaffed, people begin working on items further down the stack ranked priority list before it is clear if those items are necessary.  
* Waste Reduction: Overstaffing creates crust and slows the organization down.  
* "It is really important to me that we feel that we've deliberately understaffed every project at the company"

### **The Alpha and Beta Framework**

This framework, borrowed from finance, categorizes people and processes:

* Alpha (Outperformance): Represents creative leaps and outsized returns. High alpha people are valuable for 0-to-1 product development where an angle on a market problem is needed.  
* Beta (Volatility): Represents unpredictability and inconsistency. Processes in a business exist solely for the purpose of lowering Beta or decreasing volatility in output.  
* The Leadership Balance: Leaders must be judicious in applying process. While a payroll product should be low-beta and highly reliable, too much process can suppress the Alpha required for innovation.

### **The Pickle (Product Quality List)**

The PQL, or Pickle, is a lightweight checklist used at **Rippling** to ensure product quality before shipping.

* Cultural Symbolism: Using an idiosyncratic name like the Pickle creates a vessel for meaning that sticks in the brains of the 1,300 person R\&D organization.  
* Lowering Beta: The list provides a framework for iterating over time based on mistakes. For example, a rule was added that a product can only have one feature flag at shipment to avoid empty screens or failures during installation.  
* Standardization: It sets a comprehensive standard for what it means for a product to roll off the assembly line, ensuring consistency across locally optimized teams.

## **Combating Organizational Entropy**

Entropy, the tendency of systems toward disorder, is a constant threat to software and organizations. Every new line of code or new hire increases the entropy of the system.

* Energy Injection: The only antidote to entropy is the injection of human energy. Leaders must fight decay tooth and nail every day.  
* Mirroring Intensity: The founder CEO, [Parker Conrad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/parkerconrad), is the most intense source of energy in the company. Every concentric circle of management beyond the CEO has the potential for an order of magnitude drop-off in intensity.  
* The Role of the Executive: Executives must not buffer their teams from the intensity of the CEO. Instead, they must mirror that intensity to preserve it at the highest possible level throughout the organization.  
* Direct Feedback: Withholding feedback to avoid discomfort is viewed as fundamentally selfish. High-performance teams require uncomfortable honesty to improve.  
* "Fundamentally the most selfish thing you can do is withhold feedback from someone"

## **Success, Failure, and Product Market Fit**

The context challenges common Silicon Valley tropes regarding learning from failure and persistence.

* The Value of Winning: Seeing success is more informative than seeing failure. Success reveals how things are done correctly, which is a better heuristic for model training in one's career.  
* "You don't really learn from your mistakes, you learn from your successes."  
* Quitting and Pivoting: The mindset of trying until death is described as venture capital propaganda that serves the investor more than the entrepreneur. If a product does not have a screaming growth story by year four or five, it is often better to reset.  
* The Drug and Receptor Analogy: Markets are immutable. A startup is an experiment to see if the binding receptors for a product exist in the market. If success is not found, a company cannot market its way into existence because the receptors simply are not there.  
* Narrative Violations: Most successful businesses, like **Notion**, violate common wisdom in important ways, often succeeding based on the unique idiosyncrasies of their founders.

## **The Future of Software and AI**

**Rippling** views itself as a compound startup, building a broad platform on the people primitive. This approach has specific implications for the current technological shift toward AI.

* The High Order Bit: Product is the most important factor in a business. If the product fits the market, functions like finance, sales, and marketing become significantly easier.  
* The Data Advantage: Point solutions are in trouble because they lack sufficient data context for AI to be useful.  
* The Mine and the Shovel: Economic success in AI will likely go to those who own the models (the shovels, like **OpenAI** or **Google**) and those who own the first-party data (the mine, like **Rippling**). Middle-tier AI companies that rent both the shovel and the mine will struggle with unit economics.  
* Bundling vs. Unbundling: Software goes through cycles of bundling and unbundling. Currently, the industry is in a period of bundling because AI requires a common business data graph to perform complex tasks and correlations.
