# **2026 Canadian Tech Outlook: Briefing Document**

## **Executive Summary**

The Canadian technology landscape in 2026 is characterized by a fundamental shift away from traditional SaaS (Software as a Service) models and toward the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) with tangible resource sectors. Analysis of current trends and expert perspectives suggests that while the "AI bubble" remains a topic of debate, its impact on productivity—comparable to the agricultural revolution—is undeniable.

Critical takeaways include:

* **The Demise of Traditional SaaS:** Seat-based, per-user pricing is being replaced by outcome-based and usage-based models.  
* **Labor Market Polarization:** Task-based roles and middle management are highly vulnerable to AI automation, while the "Age of the Generalist" and high-level human leadership are ascending.  
* **Hard Asset Dominance:** Regions like Alberta are uniquely positioned to leverage energy and natural resources to backstop the massive power requirements of AI infrastructure.  
* **Financial Discipline:** Bootstrapping and capital efficiency have regained prestige, with Venture Capital (VC) facing scrutiny for "math" that often disadvantages founders and employees.  
* **Geopolitical Tech:** Defense and robotics are emerging as critical policy-led investment sectors, with robotics serving as a proxy for national warfighting capabilities.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **1\. The AI Paradigm: Beyond the Bubble**

The consensus among industry leaders is that AI is not a fleeting bubble but a structural transformation of the economy. Unlike previous speculative cycles, current AI investment is backstopped by the world’s largest corporations (e.g., Microsoft, Meta) with massive, real-world order books for power and infrastructure.

### **Productivity and Deflation**

* **Historical Parallel:** AI is likened to the agricultural revolution. Just as tractors increased productivity 10-100x and lowered food costs from 40% to 8% of household budgets, AI is expected to deliver intelligence at a negligible cost.  
* **Market Impact:** The efficiency gains from AI have already contributed to a 40% decline in the stock prices of many publicly traded SaaS companies as the market anticipates a shift away from human-intensive service models.

### **Infrastructure and Energy Demand**

* **The Power Moat:** AI’s demand for energy is real and substantial. This provides a distinct advantage to resource-rich regions like Alberta that possess the necessary power, gas, and transmission infrastructure.  
* **Data Center Volatility:** Despite the hype, the data center "gold rush" faces high failure rates. Approximately 95% of grassroots site development projects are expected to fail due to poor planning and the expiration of options on land and utilities.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **2\. The Future of Work and Labor**

The workforce is entering a period of significant displacement and redefinition. AI is moving the bottleneck of the economy away from basic intelligence and toward higher-level qualitative judgment.

### **Vulnerable and Emerging Roles**

| Role Type | Status | Outlook |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Junior/Task-Based** | At Risk | Roles focused on speed and repetition (e.g., BDRs, basic coding) are being replaced by AI agents. |
| **Middle Management** | High Risk | Execs now use AI to synthesize organizational data directly, eliminating the need for information translators. |
| **Generalists** | Rising | AI allows individuals with broad interests to action and test ideas quickly with limited resources. |
| **Human Leadership** | Essential | Soft skills, culture-building, and mission-driven inspiration remain uniquely human and more critical than ever. |

### **The "Bullshit Laptop Job" Critique**

There is a growing scrutiny of "bullshit laptop jobs"—remote roles that add little qualitative value. While remote work will persist, the competition for these roles is now global and includes AI agents, forcing a shift toward high-value, outcome-oriented work.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **3\. Financial and Company Building Trends**

The methodology for building and funding companies is undergoing a correction, prioritizing sustainability over rapid, capital-intensive growth.

* **Bootstrapping and Constraints:** Founders are increasingly realizing that "VC math" can bury good businesses under preference stacks that do not serve employees or founders. Constraints are being viewed as a catalyst for innovation and rigorous decision-making.  
* **The Micro-Business Opportunity:** AI has lowered the barrier to entry so significantly that one or two people can now build $100,000 to $1 million ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) businesses without needing external capital.  
* **Asset Moats:** Investors are pivoting toward "sleepy" industries or those with hard asset moats (energy, mining, healthcare) that are difficult to replicate and provide a defensible position against pure AI software plays.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **4\. Canadian Strategic Interests and Policy**

Canada faces specific risks and opportunities related to its resource base and regulatory environment.

### **Defense and Robotics**

* **The Robot Olympics:** By 2026, robotics will be a central theme in Canadian tech. It is predicted that international robotic competitions will serve as a proxy for a nation's defense and warfighting capabilities.  
* **Sovereignty Complex:** There is a push for a "sovereignty complex" in Canada—reviving domestic manufacturing and defense capabilities through policy-led investment.

### **Resource Security**

* **Fiat for Hard Assets:** Nations like the US and China are increasingly trading fiat currency for hard assets (rare earths, silver) to ensure long-term supply chain security. Canada, as a provider of these assets, stands to benefit if it can effectively exploit its energy and mineral resources.  
* **Nuclear Renaissance:** Canada’s historical dominance in the nuclear fuel supply chain (e.g., Saskatchewan’s uranium production) is a significant lever for the AI-driven energy transition.

### **Regulatory Challenges**

* **Healthcare Innovation:** While the US is deregulating certain medical technologies to spur innovation, Canada remains in a "gray area" regarding AI in healthcare. Clearer frameworks are needed to navigate Health Canada approvals and allow AI to address doctor shortages and mental health accessibility.  
* **Grant Dependencies:** A significant risk to Canadian founders is the reliance on milestone-based government grants. These can stifle iteration by forcing companies to adhere to multi-year plans that may no longer be relevant in a rapidly evolving market.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **5\. Key Industry Perspectives**

"In bubbles typically you don't have the largest companies in the world being the ones that are ultimately backstopping the orders with their dollars... This time you do."

"The SaaS business model is dead... People want to pay for what they use and the value that they get."

"AI can take away middle management roles... But it can't build a great culture that people want to be a part of. It can't inspire people around a mission."

"There’s never been a better time to start a company... because it gives you absolute control of your own destiny. Today there's no gatekeeping."

# **From Service to Scale – Turning Real Problems into Productized Solutions**

## **Executive Summary**

This document synthesizes key insights from a panel discussion featuring three founders — [Addy Graves](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/addy-graves-56547030) (Cashew), [Amanda Julian](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/amanda-julian-phd) (Monark), and [Lourdes Juan](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/lourdesmercedesjuan) (Knead Technologies)—moderated by [Justine Clark](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/justine-clark-2736ba84) of the DMZ. The discussion focuses on the transition from service-oriented backgrounds (consulting, market research, and non-profit work) to scalable, AI-driven technology products.

**Critical Takeaways:**

* **Services as a Strategic Foundation:** Starting with services or manual processes is an advantage, providing non-dilutive revenue, facilitating product discovery, and ensuring the product is "client-ready" before the technology is fully automated.  
* **Fundraising is Relationship-Driven:** Early-stage fundraising is less about the perfect pitch deck and more about founder conviction and building long-term relationships.  
* **AI and Proprietary Data as Moats:** In an era where AI compresses development timelines, a company’s primary defense ("moat") lies in proprietary datasets, internal benchmarking, and superior user experience (UX) design.  
* **Feature Prioritization:** Successful MVPs focus on "closing the loop" of a core process. Future development should be driven by identifying the most time-consuming manual tasks or frequent client requests.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Speaker and Company Profiles**

| Founder | Company | Core Product/Service | Current Growth Stage |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| [**Addy Graves**](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/addy-graves-56547030) | Cashew | AI-powered market research platform automating manual data analysis. | In market; Pre-seed raised. |
| [**Amanda Julian**](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/amanda-julian-phd) | Monark | AI-driven assessment and development suite for organizational leadership. | In market; Post-seed. |
| [**Lourdes Juan**](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/lourdesmercedesjuan) | Knead Technologies | Food rescue platform for municipalities and non-profits to track and scale surplus food logistics. | Early stage; Pre-seed closed late 2024; focused on product-market fit. |

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **The Strategic Transition: From Service to Product**

The panelists argued that starting as a service-based business is not a detour but a strategic advantage for building technology.

### **Advantages of the "Service-First" Model**

* **Product Discovery:** Manual service work acts as a testing ground. Founders can manually execute tasks to understand user needs before investing in expensive software development.  
* **Bootstrapping and Optionality:** Service revenue provides non-dilutive capital ("keeping the lights on"), which reduces desperate reliance on investors and gives founders more leverage during fundraising.  
* **Client Readiness:** Offering services alongside a developing product allows founders to "wrap" an unpolished MVP in professional support, making it acceptable for enterprise clients earlier in the lifecycle.

### **Identifying the "Aha Moment"**

* **Knead Technologies:** [Lourdes Juan](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/lourdesmercedesjuan) identified the problem while running a food rescue charity (Leftovers Foundation). The "aha moment" occurred when she realized that existing non-profits were using outdated manual systems (e.g., Excel) and lacked custom logistics software.  
* **Monark:** [Amanda Julian](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/amanda-julian-phd) and her co-founder, formerly management consultants, realized they could not scale their expertise at a sustainable price point for middle-level leaders. They sought a tool to buy, found none existed, and decided to build it.  
* **Cashew:** [Addy Graves](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/addy-graves-56547030) recognized that market research was "ripe for automation" after years of manually numbering PowerPoint charts and conducting manual data analysis, which made research too expensive for small businesses and inefficient for large ones.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Navigating the Fundraising Landscape**

The founders shared candid insights into the realities of raising both dilutive and non-dilutive capital.

### **Fundraising Realities and Advice**

* **The "Dating" Analogy:** [Lourdes Juan](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/lourdesmercedesjuan) characterized fundraising as a dating process, noting the emotional toll of being "ghosted" by investors. She advised founders to remain true to their vision rather than molding themselves to what they think investors want.  
* **Relationship Focus:** For pre-seed and seed rounds, investors are betting on the founding team’s ability to solve a problem rather than the immediate viability of the first solution.  
* **Patience and Timeline:** Fundraising often takes significantly longer than anticipated. [Lourdes Juan](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/lourdesmercedesjuan) noted her round took 18 months — thrice as long as expected.  
* **Non-Dilutive Capital:** Founders should not fear the reporting requirements of grants or government funding. These resources are critical for early survival and help build credibility for future raises.

### **Strategic Investor Alignment**

* **Sector Expertise:** [Amanda Julian](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/amanda-julian-phd) emphasized the importance of finding investors with specific niche expertise (e.g., HRTech or EdTech). Generalist funds often lack the synergy required to support specialized products.  
* **The Cap Table:** Founders must remember that investors will be on the cap table for years; it is essential to partner with people they "want to win with."

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Defending the Technical "Moat"**

A central challenge for AI-driven startups is defending their value proposition against claims that the same technology could be replicated quickly by others.

### **Key Defense Mechanisms**

* **Proprietary Datasets:** While the underlying AI/LLM technology might be replicable, the specific historical data and industry-specific benchmarks owned by the company are not.  
* **Bespoke Logic:** Cashew and Monark utilize "researcher logic" and internal benchmarking that pair with LLMs to provide outcomes that a generic AI tool (like Copilot) cannot achieve.  
* **User Experience and Design:** [Addy Grave](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/addy-graves-56547030) argued that design is a significant moat. A powerful backend is only valuable if it is presented in an approachable, motivating, and easy-to-understand manner for the end user.  
* **Modular and Niche Architecture:** For Knead Technologies, building a modular platform tailored to the specific logistical nuances of food rescue provides a level of customization that generic tools lack.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Product Development and Feature Prioritization**

The transition to scale requires disciplined management of the technical roadmap.

### **Prioritizing the MVP and Beyond**

* **"Closing the Loop":** Cashew’s MVP focus was strictly on reaching the end result of the research process. If a feature did not directly contribute to "closing the loop," it was discarded.  
* **Automation of Manual Pain Points:** Prioritization should target tasks that are the most time-consuming for the team or the most frequent requests from clients.  
* **The "Embarrassing" MVP:** [Amanda Julian](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/amanda-julian-phd) noted the shift from the "consultant mindset" (perfection) to the "tech mindset" (releasing unpolished products early). Getting a "borderline embarrassing" product in front of users quickly is essential for feedback.  
* **Paid Pilots:** Using a cohort of paid pilots—and in the case of Knead Technologies, using the founder's own charity as a testing ground—ensures that the roadmap is built on authentic user frustration and needs.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Ecosystem and Regional Context**

The panel highlighted the growing tech ecosystem in Calgary and its relationship with other hubs like Toronto.

* **Calgary Dynamics:** The city is noted for a "chill vibe" and a strong community of angel investors, particularly for social impact ventures.  
* **Accelerator Support:** Programs like the DMZ and Creative Destruction Lab (CDL) were cited as vital for due diligence, mentorship, and introducing founders to aligned investors.  
* **Women in Innovation:** The DMZ’s Women Innovation Summit was highlighted as a key event for female founders, offering significant investment opportunities (up to $200,000).

# Episode 018

# **The Impact of AI on Technology Organizations and Workforce Dynamics**

## **Executive Summary**

This briefing document synthesizes a high-level dialogue between [Saba El-Hilo](https://ca.linkedin.com/in/saba-elhilo) (CTO, Certn) and [Cullen Jennings](https://www.linkedin.com/in/cullen) (CTO, Cisco) regarding the transformative influence of artificial intelligence (AI) on technical leadership, organizational structure, and the workforce. The analysis highlights a shift from fully remote to hybrid "centers of excellence" to mitigate the friction of time zones. While AI significantly accelerates development and prototyping, it is increasingly viewed as a tool that replaces entry-level tasks — specifically in Quality Assurance (QA) — rather than entire senior roles. However, the integration of AI introduces new challenges, including the "exhaustion" of managing AI agents, the "arms race" of deep fake fraud, and the limitations of AI context windows in large-scale legacy architectures.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Career Leadership and Strategic Decision-Making**

The leaders emphasize that technical success is as much about psychological resilience and timing as it is about engineering.

* **Core Career Philosophy:**  
  * **Ambition and Risk:** Success requires the courage to take risks and not be deterred by "no." Staying in a "status quo" 9-to-5 role is often riskier than entrepreneurship.  
  * **Operational Principles:** Effective leadership is defined by showing up, paying attention, telling the truth, and remaining detached from specific results to allow for pivot and risk-taking.  
* **The Criticality of Timing:**  
  * Timing is identified as a primary factor in business failure. Launching too early or too late can negate technical superiority.  
  * Leaders must look for "road signs" and roadmaps to determine when a market is ready for a specific innovation.  
* **Project Management Pitfalls:**  
  * **Blind Optimism:** A recurring failure in tech leadership is the tendency to underestimate timelines by failing to account for "unknown unknowns," particularly during large-scale migrations.  
  * **Balancing Unknowns:** Over-indexing on potential unknowns can lead to paralysis, necessitating a balance between optimism and realistic contingency planning.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Workforce Evolution and Global Talent Dynamics**

The discussion reveals a maturation of the remote work model, moving away from radical decentralization toward strategic regional hubs.

### **The Shift to Hybrid Work**

Both organizations are moving toward "centers of excellence" or hybrid models.

* **The Time Zone Problem:** Time zones are described as the "enemy of all work." Distributed teams across too many regions (e.g., 272 locations for Cisco’s collaboration group) lead to burnout and "ridiculous" delays where simple decisions take weeks to schedule.  
* **The Hub Model:** The goal is to consolidate full teams (designers, developers, and managers) in the same time zone to accelerate the feedback loop and increase speed.

### **Labor Arbitrage and Global Talent Quality**

The value of global talent is measured in "dollars per work" rather than "dollars per hour."

| Region | Cost Profile | Quality/Context |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Silicon Valley** | Highest | Access to specialized talent and acquired startups. |
| **India** | Rising Rapidly | Costs per unit of work have tripled in seven years. |
| **Ireland** | Low (Adjusted) | High tax credits (35% back) and high university quality. |
| **Canada** | Medium | Strong university pipeline, high quality, beneficial tax credits (SR\&ED). |
| **Poland/Slovakia** | Lower | Attractive cost-to-quality ratio. |

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **AI in the Technical Organization**

AI is reshaping how software is built, tested, and managed, though it introduces new psychological and metric-related complexities.

### **Impact on Headcount and Roles**

* **Junior Developers:** AI is frequently compared to a "junior developer." While some argue this replaces entry-level roles, others suggest junior engineers are actually *accelerated* more by AI than seniors.  
* **QA and Support:** The role of dedicated Quality Assurance (QA) is viewed as increasingly obsolete. AI agents can now handle testing in production and lower-level environments, making quality the responsibility of the developer.  
* **Role Democratization:** AI allows non-technical roles (CS, Product, Design) to perform technical tasks. One strategy is to embed developers into CS teams to drive automation where it is traditionally slow-moving.

### **AI Implementation and Productivity**

* **Workflow Integration:** AI (Cursor, Claude, Codex) is used heavily for code reviews, testing, and prototyping. At Certn, AI usage is now a performance metric for developers.  
* **The "Joy of Work" Concern:** Shifting from problem-solving to "managing AI agents" may reduce the "dopamine high" and mastery associated with coding.  
* **The Exhaustion Factor:** Managing multiple AI agents for several hours can be more mentally taxing and stressful for engineers than traditional manual programming.  
* **Efficiency Metrics:** There is currently no valid metric for programmer efficiency, and AI has not solved this. Attempts to measure it usually result in "gaming the metric."

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Technical Architecture and Data Sovereignty**

Architecture decisions are increasingly driven by geopolitical and regulatory requirements rather than just technical scalability.

* **Monolith vs. Microservices:**  
  * **Certn's Monolith:** A monolith architecture was chosen for simplicity and ease of deployment across different regions (data residency).  
  * **Cisco’s Microservices:** Large-scale systems like Webex use hundreds of microservices, which can make spinning up new regional data centers complicated.  
* **Data Sovereignty:** Global clients (Europe, Australia, Middle East) increasingly require data to be stored and processed within their specific regions.  
  * **The "Australian Model":** Australia has exceptionally tight regulations, sometimes requiring that even infrastructure management be performed by personnel physically located in-region.  
* **The Context Window Limit:** Large legacy codebases (e.g., Cisco IOS with 20+ years of history) are too large to fit into current AI context windows, limiting the effectiveness of AI tools in those environments.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## **Cybersecurity: The New AI Frontier**

AI is creating a lopsided "arms race" that currently favors attackers over defenders.

* **Deep Fakes in Hiring:** Organizations are seeing "convincing" audio and video fakes during interviews. Detecting these requires looking for subtle "flickering" or asking personal, non-scripted questions to stump the AI.  
* **Single-Shot Cloning:** Current technology can clone a human voice with less than nine seconds of audio, allowing attackers to create authentic-sounding fraudulent calls or videos.  
* **The Defender’s Dilemma:** Attackers only need to win once; defenders must win every time. Furthermore, attackers use the defenders' own detection systems to test and refine their evasion techniques.  
* **Recommended Countermeasures:**  
  * **Process over Technology:** Relying solely on deep fake detection is a losing strategy. Companies must implement strict processes for financial transactions (e.g., POs and cash transfers).  
  * **Identity Verification:** Strengthening multi-factor authentication and identity verification is more critical than trying to detect 100% of "spam" or fakes.  
  * **Skepticism:** Users must treat electronic audio and video with the same level of inherent skepticism as suspicious "prince from a foreign land" emails.
